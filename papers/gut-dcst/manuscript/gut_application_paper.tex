\section{Abstract}\label{abstract}

Inflammatory bowel disease (IBD) provides a demanding test case for a
broader systems-microbiology problem: how to compress high-dimensional
gut community profiles into sample-level states that remain
interpretable across cohorts. IBD has repeatedly been associated with
heterogeneous dysbiosis, including loss or redistribution of obligate
anaerobic commensals and expansion of facultative or
inflammation-associated taxa, but those shifts are not naturally
summarized by one portable sample label. Gut enterotypes provide one
such summary, but they are clustering-defined constructs whose labels
depend on choices about data transformation, distance metric, clustering
model, and the number of clusters. We therefore derived absorb-policy
dominant-community state types (dCSTs), a deterministic rank-based
representation of retained-feature dominance, in 24,421
quality-controlled American Gut Project (AGP) stool samples from a
24,566-sample local ASV table whose taxonomy was reassigned with the
same SILVA workflow used for the external validation datasets, using
AGP primarily as a large ecological structure-learning cohort rather
than as a source of diagnosis-grade IBD case definitions. An
exploratory AGP condition screen prioritized self-reported IBD for
follow-up, with multistate label-level associations that included
enriched \taxon{[Eubacterium]~eligens~group~eligens} and
\taxon{Alistipes} states, depletion of \taxon{Segatella}, and recurring
deeper \taxon{Bacteroides~dorei}-linked contexts. The primary
disease-association test then froze the AGP-derived
hierarchy and mapped external cohorts with stronger IBD diagnosis
metadata into it. Halfvarson 2017 provided the clearest stool-based
support: at the sample level, transferred signals were significant in
pooled IBD, Crohn disease, and ulcerative colitis and were centered on
depleted \taxon{Segatella} states together with enriched
\taxon{Bacteroides~dorei} in pooled IBD and ulcerative colitis, but
after one-sample-per-subject sensitivity those direct-transfer
disease-versus-control contrasts attenuated. Rebuilt-cohort
ulcerative-colitis and within-Crohn location contrasts then provided
the clearest subject-aware signal in Halfvarson. HMP2 / IBDMDB mapped
only to depth 1 in the current mucosal-only slice and did not retain
corrected rebuilt or transferred labels; Gevers 2014 mapped well into
the hierarchy but likewise did not yield corrected transferred labels,
providing pediatric and mucosal boundaries on portability. These
results support
dCSTs as
an interpretable, portable representation of established heterogeneous
IBD dysbiosis rather than as evidence for new microbial biomarkers.
Exploratory within-disease follow-up in Halfvarson further identified a
rebuilt-mode depth-2
\taxon{Bacteroides~dorei}/\taxon{Faecalibacterium~prausnitzii}
contrast between ileal-involved and colonic-only Crohn disease
(sample-level OR 0.18, 95\% CI 0.074 to 0.41), with bootstrap
one-sample-per-subject recurrence approaching a pragmatic 50\%
subject-level stability threshold, whereas the corresponding
AGP-transferred labels did not remain subject-stable.
Exploratory AGP analyses for IBS, autoimmune disease, acid reflux, and
seasonal allergies are retained in the supplement.
Taken together, the framework is best viewed as a reusable
representation layer for cross-cohort microbiome analysis and for future
integration with richer clinical or multi-omics follow-up, rather than
as a stand-alone diagnostic test.

\textbf{Keywords:} gut microbiome; inflammatory bowel disease;
dysbiosis; enterotypes; dominant-community state types; Bayesian
modeling

\section{Introduction}\label{introduction}

For common gut disorders, clinical diagnoses do not map neatly onto one
standalone molecular or laboratory test. IBD diagnosis integrates
clinical presentation with biochemical, stool, \mbox{endoscopic},
imaging, and histologic evidence rather than relying on one reference standard
(\citeproc{ref-Maaser2019_ECCO_ESGAR_IBD_diagnostics}{Maaser et al.
2019}). IBS is even more symptom-defined, with no validated diagnostic
biomarker and a guideline-supported positive diagnostic strategy
(\citeproc{ref-Lacy2021_ACG_IBS}{Lacy et al. 2021}). This creates a
substantial interpretive challenge for microbiome studies:
disease-associated community shifts are repeatedly observed, but they are
heterogeneous and difficult to translate into sample-level ecological
labels that can be compared across cohorts.

In IBD, the existence of microbiome dysbiosis is no longer the central
question. Landmark studies have shown that IBD is not characterized by
one universal microbial signature, but often involves reduced diversity,
depletion or redistribution of butyrate-associated obligate anaerobes,
and expansion of facultative or inflammation-associated taxa
(\citeproc{ref-Frank2007_IBD}{Frank et al. 2007};
\citeproc{ref-Gevers2014_CD}{Gevers et al. 2014};
\citeproc{ref-LloydPrice2019_IBD}{Lloyd-Price et al. 2019};
\citeproc{ref-Sokol2008_Fprausnitzii}{Sokol et al. 2008};
\citeproc{ref-Manichanh2006_CD}{Manichanh et al. 2006}). These
observations motivate a practical representation question rather than a
new diagnostic-test claim: can established but heterogeneous
IBD-associated microbiome shifts be summarized into transparent
sample-level dominance states that can be compared across cohorts?

Gut enterotypes were introduced to provide such labels by compressing
high-dimensional gut abundance profiles into a small number of
clustering-defined configurations
(\citeproc{ref-Arumugam2011_enterotypes}{Arumugam et al. 2011}). That
abstraction can be informative for broad community structure, but it has
remained controversial precisely because the labels are clustering
constructs: assignments depend on data transformation, distance metric,
initialization, model class, and the chosen number of clusters
(\citeproc{ref-Koren2013_enterotypes}{Koren et al. 2013};
\citeproc{ref-Knights2014_rethinking_enterotypes}{Knights et al. 2014};
\citeproc{ref-Costea2018_enterotypes}{Costea et al. 2018}). Those
choices can make it hard to determine what a label means biologically
for a particular sample or whether labels from different analyses are
directly comparable.

We use dominant-community state types (dCSTs) as a deterministic
rank-based framework for naming sample-level gut ecological states. A
dCST is defined by the within-sample ordering of retained taxa. At depth
1, the state is assigned by the dominant retained taxon; at depth 2, it
is refined by the ordered pair of the two most abundant retained taxa.
Because assignment depends only on within-sample rank order, the same
label is recovered from counts, proportions, or any sample-wide
rescaling that preserves taxon order within a sample. We use
\emph{dominance-lineage} to denote the ordered path defined by a
depth-\(\ell\) dCST label as the hierarchy is refined across ranks. The
term refers only to within-sample dominance order and does not imply
phylogenetic relatedness. The dCST construction provides a
transparent way to ask whether IBD-associated dysbiosis can be summarized
as dominance states that remain interpretable after covariate adjustment,
hierarchical refinement, Bayesian sparse-state follow-up, and external
validation. Accordingly, a \taxon{Bacteroides}-dominant dCST should be
read as a rank-order state rather than as a claim of absolute
\taxon{Bacteroides} expansion. The construction is illustrated in
Figure 1.

This application therefore centers on IBD because it offers a demanding
external test of whether dCSTs can organize gut microbiome dysbiosis
into interpretable sample-level states that survive cross-cohort
transfer. At the same time, IBD is not one disease: in routine clinical
practice it includes at least Crohn disease (CD) and ulcerative colitis
(UC), with the further possibility of clinically meaningful subtypes
within each. AGP itself does not provide diagnosis-grade separation of
CD, UC, disease activity, or treatment exposure, so the AGP
self-reported IBD dCST association analysis is used here only as an
auxiliary exploratory prioritization signal. The main systems question
in this manuscript is whether an AGP-derived dCST-hierarchy provides a
portable state vocabulary when transferred to external cohorts with
reliable CD/UC diagnosis. We therefore emphasize Halfvarson 2017 as the
main stool-based CD/UC validation cohort, HMP2 / IBDMDB as a clinically
rich IBD-focused complement, and Gevers 2014 as a Crohn-focused
external anchor. IBS, autoimmune disease, acid reflux, and seasonal
allergies were also screened in AGP, but they are treated here as
exploratory supplementary applications. In this study we use the absorb low-frequency
dCST-policy, which merges very small dominance patterns into larger
named states rather than pooling them into one catch-all rare category.
Pure-policy dCST results serve as a baseline comparison in the
supplement.

For a clinically oriented IBD reader, a second question follows
naturally once disease-versus-control transfer is established: can the
same framework recover structure \emph{within} IBD when the validation
metadata are rich enough? We therefore treat the external CD/UC
case-control analyses as primary and a smaller set of within-disease
Halfvarson follow-ups as exploratory clinical extensions. Those
follow-ups ask whether dCSTs can organize Montreal-classified Crohn
location, Montreal-classified UC extent, and visit-level fecal
calprotectin burden through a pragmatic high-burden split sensitivity,
while remaining explicit that such
within-disease contrasts are not anchored by AGP diagnosis labels and
need stronger subject-level scrutiny than the broader disease-versus-
healthy comparisons.

Figure 2 summarizes the logic that motivates the main analysis. In the
large AGP training cohort, the exploratory depthwise screen highlighted
self-reported IBD as the clearest candidate for follow-up, with
Cramer's V increasing from 0.24 at depth 1 to 0.31 at depth 4
(Figure 2A). The
clinically relevant test is then whether the AGP-derived dCST-hierarchy
transfers to cohorts with reliable IBD diagnosis. Figure 2B therefore

filled markers show the strongest sample-level corrected signal in each

AGP-derived label transfer retained corrected sample-level depth-1
signal for pooled IBD, Crohn disease, and ulcerative colitis through
depleted \taxon{Segatella} states, with concurrent
\taxon{Bacteroides~dorei} enrichment in pooled IBD and ulcerative
colitis, but those direct-transfer disease-versus-control contrasts
attenuated after one-sample-per-subject sensitivity. In HMP2 /

\section{Results}\label{cohort-overview}

\subsection{AGP-derived dominance-state hierarchy and exploratory prioritization}\label{agp-derived-dominance-state-hierarchy-and-exploratory-prioritization}

The discovery stage asked whether interpretable dominance states could
organize large-scale gut ecological variation in 24,421
quality-controlled AGP stool samples and thereby provide a stable
structure for external disease-focused transfer. The absorb dCST
hierarchy contained 50 named states at depth 1, 158 at depth 2, 219 at
depth 3, and 221 at depth 4. The largest depth-1 states were
\taxon{Bacteroides~dorei} (8,734 samples),
\taxon{Faecalibacterium~prausnitzii} (3,703), \taxon{Segatella} sp.
(2,557), \taxon{Akkermansia~muciniphila} (875), and
\taxon{Agathobacter~faecis} (846). The twelve most common depth-1
absorb dCST dominance-lineages covered 79.8\% of the cohort
(Table~\ref{tab:agp-depth1-landscape}), showing that a
limited number of dominant ecological configurations accounted for most
samples even after low-frequency patterns were absorbed into larger
named states. These common depth-1 states define the main vocabulary
carried into the direct external-transfer analyses below. Supplementary
Table S4 shows representative depth-2 expansions of four common
depth-1 parents that recur later in the IBD interpretation, illustrating
that broad parents such as \taxon{Bacteroides~dorei},
\taxon{Faecalibacterium~prausnitzii}, and \taxon{Segatella} sp. contain
several recurrent secondary contexts rather than a single homogeneous
subtype. Some retained-feature labels also carried low-resolution or
potential non-gut annotations outside the top common states, including
\taxon{Bacillota} phy., \taxon{Acinetobacter} sp., \taxon{Pseudomonas}
sp., and \taxon{Corynebacterium~pseudotuberculosis} at depth 1 and a
small number of higher-level Bacillota and Enterobacterales lineages at
deeper levels (Table~\ref{tab:agp-depth1-landscape}; Supplementary
Table S2). We
retained these labels in the unsupervised hierarchy for transparent
accounting, but we do not interpret them as disease-relevant gut states
in the IBD results.

An exploratory depthwise AGP condition screen then asked which
participant-reported conditions were most clearly organized by the
hierarchy.
IBD again provided the strongest candidate signal: across depths 1 to
4, Cramer's V increased from 0.24 to 0.31 in self-reported IBD,
compared with 0.17 to 0.23 in autoimmune disease, 0.09 to 0.14 in
seasonal allergies, 0.08 to 0.12 in IBS, and 0.06 to 0.12 in acid
reflux. Within AGP, the corresponding label-level follow-up was
multistate rather than dominated by one broad parent lineage. Adjusted
follow-up repeatedly recovered enriched
\taxon{[Eubacterium]~eligens~group~eligens},
\taxon{Alistipes} sp., \taxon{Blautia} sp., and
\taxon{Bifidobacterium~breve}, together with depleted
\taxon{Segatella} sp. and recurring deeper
\taxon{Bacteroides~dorei}-linked contexts (Supplementary Figure S1;

coarse AGP antibiotic-status sensitivity retained the main directions of
those leading labels. Because AGP does not support diagnosis-grade CD/UC
subtype inference, we use that discovery-stage result mainly to
prioritize the main external IBD transfer analysis. Exploratory AGP
analyses for IBS, autoimmune disease, acid reflux, and seasonal
allergies are summarized in the supplement (Supplementary Table S5).

\subsection{Transfer of AGP-derived dominance states to clinically annotated IBD cohorts}\label{transfer-of-agp-derived-dominance-states-to-clinically-annotated-ibd-cohorts}

The main inferential test asked whether AGP-derived absorb dCST labels
remained associated with clinician-annotated IBD case definitions when
they were applied unchanged to external cohorts. Rebuilt-cohort analyses
were retained as a secondary comparison to distinguish broader
cross-cohort reproducibility from exact AGP-label portability. Primary
validation retained all eligible samples after cohort-specific label
filtering, raw library-quality control, and AGP-overlap restriction; in
the repeated-sample cohorts, one-sample-per-subject sensitivity retained
the first available sample per participant, and Supplementary Table S12
adds a bootstrap one-sample-per-subject consensus across 1,000
resamples. Supplementary Table S11D reports the resulting sample and
subject counts, and Supplementary Tables S14A
to S18H provide dataset-specific appendix tables for rebuilt-cohort and
direct AGP-derived associations across depths 1 to 4, each with
frequentist and Bayesian effect-size summaries. Table~\ref{tab:depth1-transfer-landscape}
shows how the main external comparisons intersected with the common
AGP depth-1 states in Table~\ref{tab:agp-depth1-landscape}.

Halfvarson 2017 provided the clearest stool-based support for this
transfer framework. After filtering, the pooled branch contained 533
stool samples from 118 subjects: 479 pooled IBD-case samples from 109
case subjects and 54 healthy-control samples from 9 healthy subjects.
The subtype-resolved branches contained 200 Crohn-case samples from 49
case subjects and 279 UC-case samples from 60 case subjects, each
compared against the same 54 healthy-control samples from 9 control
subjects. Because 99 of the 118 pooled-branch subjects contributed
repeated samples and only 9 healthy subjects anchored the pooled control
arm, Halfvarson is the clearest setting in which to distinguish
sample-level replication from subject-level sensitivity.
When samples were mapped directly onto the AGP-derived dCST-hierarchy,
the pooled IBD-versus-healthy, Crohn-disease-versus-healthy, and
ulcerative-colitis-versus-healthy comparisons all retained q < 0.001.
As summarized in Table~\ref{tab:depth1-transfer-landscape}, those
depth-1 transfer results fell squarely within the common AGP states from
Table~\ref{tab:agp-depth1-landscape}: pooled IBD, Crohn disease, and
ulcerative colitis all showed strong depletion of \taxon{Segatella} sp.
together with depletion of \taxon{Akkermansia~muciniphila}, while
\taxon{Bacteroides~dorei} was enriched in pooled IBD and ulcerative
colitis and remained more weakly but still corrected-significant in
Crohn disease. Rebuilt-cohort validation in the same dataset was also
positive for pooled IBD, Crohn disease, and ulcerative colitis after
multiple-testing correction. The leading rebuilt labels again differed
by comparison but were coherent: depletion of \taxon{Segatella} sp. in
pooled IBD and Crohn disease together with enrichment of
\taxon{Bacteroides~dorei} in ulcerative colitis. In the
subject-level sensitivity analysis, however, those strong sample-level
BH-significant disease-versus-control contrasts no longer retained
\(q < 0.05\) after collapsing to one sample per subject. The direct
AGP-derived transfer contrasts attenuated most strongly: bootstrap
one-sample-per-subject consensus for the leading transferred labels was
only 9.3\% in pooled IBD, 9.6\% in Crohn disease, and 8.5\% in
ulcerative colitis. Rebuilt-cohort disease-versus-control signal also
weakened under subject collapse, but rebuilt ulcerative colitis remained
the clearest residual subject-aware pattern, with first-sample
\(q = 0.075\), bootstrap recurrence 58.3\%, and median bootstrap OR
5.05 (empirical 95\% interval 2.32 to 11.47; Supplementary Table S12),
rather than a second formally corrected subject-level-positive result.
Exploratory sample-level Halfvarson clinical stratification follow-up
then asked whether the same dominance-state framework could resolve
within-disease heterogeneity. The clearest additional structure lay
within Crohn disease rather than along a simple UC extent split:
extensive-versus-distal UC produced only suggestive rebuilt-cohort
shifts and no corrected AGP-derived transfer signal, whereas
ileal-involved Crohn disease was differentiated from colonic-only
Crohn disease by both rebuilt and transferred labels. Here
``ileal-involved'' follows the Montreal location schema and combines
L1/L1+L4 and L3/L3+L4, whereas ``colonic-only'' corresponds to L2
(\citeproc{ref-Satsangi2006_Montreal}{Satsangi et al. 2006}). In direct
AGP-derived transfer, ileal-involved Crohn disease was marked at depth 1
by depletion of \taxon{Faecalibacterium~prausnitzii}
(OR 0.12, 95\% CI 0.028 to 0.39, q = 0.0010) relative to colonic-only
Crohn disease, while deeper AGP-transferred labels were directional but
not correction-significant. Rebuilt follow-up was stronger still: the
depth-2
\emph{Bacteroides~dorei}/\emph{Faecalibacterium~prausnitzii}
contrast carried a sample-level OR of 0.18 (95\% CI 0.074 to 0.41,
q = 4.4e-05), and the deeper rebuilt contrasts remained aligned with
the same \taxon{Bacteroides}-centered/\taxon{Escherichia-Shigella}-
linked axis. Biologically, that Crohn-location signal recovers an
established ileal-Crohn pattern of
\taxon{Faecalibacterium} depletion with
\taxon{Escherichia-Shigella}/AIEC-associated enrichment rather than a
generic IBD-versus-healthy effect
(\citeproc{ref-DarfeuilleMichaud2004_AIEC_ileal_CD}{Darfeuille-Michaud
et al. 2004}).
A sample-level Crohn calprotectin split at
250~\textmu g/g, chosen here as a conservative high-inflammatory-burden
threshold rather than a remission cut-off
(\citeproc{ref-Walsham2016_calprotectin}{Walsham and Sherwood 2016}),
showed additional but narrower within-Crohn structure: the leading
rebuilt depth-3 label had OR 0.145 (95\% CI 0.015 to 0.68, q = 0.044),
whereas the AGP-derived transfer labels were directional but not
correction-significant in the canonical rerun, without the broader
multi-depth consistency seen for Crohn location. We therefore retain the 250
\textmu g/g split only as a clinically legible high-inflammatory-burden
sensitivity layer (Supplementary Table S13H). A small threshold sweep across
100, 150, 200, and 250~\textmu g/g did not show a uniformly stable
signal at all lower cut-offs: the clearest rebuilt signal remained
concentrated at the higher-burden end of that range, whereas the
AGP-transfer enterobacterial signal recurred at 100, 200, and
250~\textmu g/g (Supplementary Table S13I and Supplementary Figure~S2).
These within-disease contrasts were exploratory and were BH-corrected
within each comparison's label panel rather than across the three
follow-up questions.
Subject-aware sensitivity reinforced that ranking. UC extent remained
weak after retaining one sample per subject and showed essentially no
bootstrap consensus across depths. Crohn location retained the
strongest within-disease subject-level support, but mainly in rebuilt
mode: the depth-2
\emph{Bacteroides~dorei}/\emph{Faecalibacterium~prausnitzii}
contrast remained significant after the first-sample-per-subject
sensitivity pass (q = 0.042) and crossed \(q < 0.05\) in 49.6\% of
bootstrap resamples, which approaches but does not fully cross a
pragmatic 50\% working threshold for subject-level stability, whereas
the corresponding AGP-derived transfer labels attenuated under
subject-level sensitivity. The calprotectin split behaved more like a
visit-level modifier: its sample-level rebuilt depth-3 and AGP-derived
transfer depth-2 signals were not stable after
one-sample-per-subject sensitivity, and only the rebuilt depth-2 label
showed modest bootstrap recurrence (11.4\%) (Supplementary Table S13D).
We therefore treat Halfvarson as the strongest
current sample-level stool-based support for dCST-hierarchy transfer
overall, with the clearest current subject-level stability in
rebuilt ulcerative colitis, which crosses the working 50\% bootstrap
stability threshold, and rebuilt Crohn location, which approaches it
(Supplementary Tables S14A to S14H).

HMP2 / IBDMDB provided a different but still important validation
setting: richer clinical IBD metadata, but a current PRIME slice that is
mucosal and longitudinal rather than stool-equivalent to AGP. After
filtering, the pooled branch contained 166 mucosal gut-site samples from
81 subjects: 121 pooled IBD-case samples from 59 case subjects and 45
healthy-control samples from 22 control subjects. Within that branch,
the subtype-resolved comparisons comprised 81 Crohn-case samples from 37
subjects and 40 UC-case samples from 22 subjects. Because 67 of the 81
pooled-branch subjects contributed repeated samples, HMP2 is also best
read as a sample-level primary analysis with explicit subject-level
sensitivity. In the parent HMP2 study, stool, biopsy, and blood were
all collected, but the PRIME-derived validation slice used here
contributed rectal, ileal, and other colonic mucosal samples only, with
no stool samples in the local analysis matrix. Under the canonical
shared-taxonomy AGP reference, that current mucosal slice mapped into
the AGP hierarchy at depth 1 only and did not retain corrected rebuilt
or direct-transfer pooled IBD signal; the strongest rebuilt pooled
contrast was the directional but not corrected
\taxon{Bacteroides}\dcstsep\taxon{Faecalibacterium} child
(q = 0.24), while the direct-transfer branch was null
(Supplementary Table S12; Supplementary Tables S15A to S15H). We
therefore use HMP2 primarily as a clinically curated but ecologically
non-equivalent portability boundary rather than as a second positive
stool-style transfer replication. HMP2
within-disease stratification is not emphasized here because layering
subtype, mucosal site, and repeated-subject structure on top of an
already mucosal-only slice leaves strata that are too sparse and too
ecologically confounded to serve as a clear analogue of the Halfvarson
stool-based within-disease follow-up.

Gevers 2014 served as the main Crohn-focused external anchor. Samples
mapped well into the AGP-derived dCST-hierarchy, but neither rebuilt-cohort nor
direct AGP-derived label-transfer tests retained corrected absorb dCST
dominance-lineage signal in the current pediatric
Crohn-disease-versus-healthy comparison. After filtering, the Gevers
branch contained 396 stool samples from 363 subjects: 363 Crohn-case
samples from 331 case subjects and 33 healthy-control samples from 32
control subjects, with only 8 subjects contributing repeated samples.
Exploratory medication follow-up in this same Gevers stool branch did
not rescue case-control transfer when we restricted the analysis to
samples explicitly flagged antibiotic-negative, mesalamine-negative, or
steroid-negative: the best rebuilt q-values in those subsets ranged from
0.067 to 0.109 and the best direct-transfer q-values from 0.486 to
0.774. Within Gevers Crohn cases, antibiotic exposure was the clearest
medication-associated split, with depth-1 transferred
\taxon{Bacteroides} depletion (q = 0.001) and depth-2 transferred
\taxon{Escherichia-Shigella}\dcstsep\taxon{Bifidobacterium} enrichment
(q = 0.003) in antibiotic-positive samples. Mesalamine follow-up was
weaker, and steroids yielded only a rebuilt depth-2
\taxon{Faecalibacterium prausnitzii}\dcstsep\taxon{Dialister invisus}
signal (q = 0.045). That result does not erase the broader AGP or
Halfvarson signal, but it sets an important boundary on portability
across new-onset pediatric Crohn disease and a more specialized cohort
ecology. Good mapping was therefore not sufficient for corrected label
portability in a setting that differed from AGP and Halfvarson in age
structure, disease stage, and cohort composition, and medication alone
does not appear sufficient to explain that boundary. We therefore
interpret the present IBD-focused external evidence as strongest for
AGP-derived dCST-hierarchy transfer in Halfvarson and
boundary-setting rather than positive in HMP2 and Gevers (Figure 3;
Supplementary Table S11D; Supplementary Tables S13E and S16A to S16H).
Non-IBD secondary external comparisons such as PRJEB84421 and Jacobs
2023 IBS 250bp are summarized in the supplement rather than in this
IBD-focused subsection (Supplementary Tables S17A to S18H).

Direct transfer also showed case-control mapping differences in
Halfvarson, especially at deeper depths, where IBD cases mapped more
often than controls. We therefore treat AGP-derived label transfer as
strongest when both the association and the mapping diagnostics are
favorable, and we avoid interpreting nonmapping as biological absence
(Supplementary Tables S12 and S13). Exploratory AGP analyses for IBS,
autoimmune disease, acid reflux, and seasonal allergies, together with
the current Jacobs IBS validation results, are summarized in the
supplement rather than in the main text.

\section{Materials and Methods}\label{methods}

The discovery stage used the local AGP QIIME 2 feature table rather
than the older PRIME-derived taxon table
(\citeproc{ref-McDonald2018_AGP}{McDonald et al. 2018};
\citeproc{ref-Quast2013_SILVA}{Quast et al. 2013}) to derive the absorb
dCST hierarchy and to provide an exploratory condition screen that
could prioritize external disease-focused follow-up. AGP md5 ASV
identifiers were matched back to sequences from the corresponding
redbiom 100 nt V4 context, taxonomy was reassigned with the same
DADA2/SILVA workflow used for the external validation datasets, and the
resulting compact taxon labels were collapsed to a sample-by-taxon AGP
abundance matrix. The local AGP source table contained 24,566 samples.
Samples with total library size below 1,000 reads were excluded,
removing 145 samples. Taxa were then retained only if they reached a
count of at least 2 in at least 5\% of the retained samples,
corresponding to a prevalence threshold of 1,221 samples in this
cohort. This feature filter retained 340 taxa, and no additional
samples became all-zero after feature filtering, leaving 24,421 samples
for the discovery analysis. Age was available for 23,310 samples, sex
for 23,534, and BMI for 22,956, producing 22,001 complete cases for the
absorb-policy dCST dominance-lineage follow-up models. Because dCST construction was
applied after this feature-retention step, all dCST labels should be
interpreted as retained-feature dominance labels. They do not imply
dominance over every raw feature removed during preprocessing. Feature
labels use the deepest informative SILVA annotation available for each
retained feature, so labels can refer to species, genera, families,
orders, or unresolved features such as \emph{Unassigned}.

Condition flags were derived directly from the AGP \texttt{Phenotype}
metadata field by fixed substring matching. The five outcome variables
screened in AGP were coded from the phrases
\texttt{irritable bowel syndrome},
\texttt{inflammatory bowel disease}, \texttt{autoimmune},
\texttt{acid reflux}, and \texttt{seasonal allergies}. For each outcome,
samples without the corresponding outcome-defining phrase served as
controls, and outcome flags were therefore not mutually exclusive. In
the filtered cohort, case counts were 4,053 for IBS, 1,238 for IBD,
2,734 for autoimmune disease, 3,444 for acid reflux, and 9,203 for
seasonal allergies; corresponding complete-case counts after requiring
age, sex, and BMI were 3,815, 1,177, 2,586, 3,237, and 8,499,
respectively (Supplementary Table S1). These AGP outcome fields were
used only for exploratory discovery prioritization and sensitivity
analysis. They were not treated as diagnosis-grade clinical case
definitions.
In particular, the AGP discovery cohort did not provide IBD subtype,
disease activity, medication exposure, or treatment histories in a form
that supported defensible clinical CD/UC subtype modeling. The main
disease-association claims in this manuscript therefore come from
external cohorts with stronger IBD diagnosis metadata. The coarser AGP
\texttt{Antibiotic\_Status} field was available for most
covariate-complete AGP samples and was used in sensitivity analyses that
either added antibiotic status as a categorical covariate or excluded
samples reporting antibiotic use in the previous week, month, or six
months (Supplementary Table S8).

Absorb-policy dCSTs were constructed from the within-sample dominance
order (Figure 1). A depth-1 state is defined by the dominant retained
taxon, and deeper states are defined by recursively appending the
second-ranked, third-ranked, and subsequent retained taxa. Because
assignment depends only on within-sample rank order, the label is
unchanged whether the input is expressed as raw counts, relative
abundances, or any other sample-wide rescaling that preserves the order
of taxa within each sample. We used the \texttt{absorb} low-frequency
dCST-policy throughout. At depth 1, only dominance patterns observed in at
least \(n_0 = 50\) samples were kept as named states. Samples in smaller
patterns were reassigned to the retained state with the largest abundance
among the retained taxa for that sample, with deterministic tie handling.
We then refined the hierarchy with \(n_0 = 25, 25,\) and \(25\) for
depths 2 through 4, respectively. At each depth, the same rule was
applied within the current parent state, producing ordered dCST
dominance-lineages.
Depths 1 through 4 are treated as the primary results in this
manuscript. This paper-facing \(n_0\) schedule was chosen to keep the
deeper retained lineage sets large enough for stable prevalence
estimation and cross-cohort transfer while still allowing ecological
refinement beyond the dominant parent state. We did not perform a full
multi-schedule sensitivity sweep in the manuscript; the
absorb-versus-pure comparison reported in Supplementary Table S9 shows
that the leading IBD-associated lineages are recovered under both
policies, and a finer-grained \(n_0\) schedule remains a natural
resolution-increasing sensitivity analysis in further methodological
work on the dCST framework.

The pure-policy dCST hierarchy without absorption served as a baseline
comparison and is summarized in the supplement. The inferential workflow
reported here is therefore absorb based from the cohort overview through
outcome-specific follow-up.

To ask whether a condition was associated with the overall absorb dCST
dominance-lineage distribution at a given depth, we first applied Fisher's exact test to
the condition-by-state table for that depth. We screened panels with
10,000 Monte Carlo draws and reran the strongest panels with 100,000
draws to obtain finer p-value resolution. We summarize the magnitude of
these depth-level associations with Cramer's V. We then performed
label-level follow-up in two parallel ways for IBD, IBS, autoimmune
disease, acid reflux, and seasonal allergies. The conventional analysis
used Fisher tests and logistic regression adjusted for age, sex, and BMI.
The Bayesian analysis used two complementary models. For unadjusted
label-level contrasts we used a beta-binomial prevalence model with a
Jeffreys prior, \(\mathrm{Beta}(0.5, 0.5)\), summarized by the posterior
median odds ratio, 95\% credible interval, and posterior probability
that the odds ratio exceeded 1. For adjusted analyses we fit Bayesian
logistic regression models with \texttt{arm::bayesglm} using the same
covariates as the frequentist regression and the package's default
weakly informative prior. Those adjusted Bayesian fits were summarized
by the exponentiated coefficient estimate, an approximate 95\% credible
interval based on the posterior standard error, and \(\Pr(\beta > 0)\)
for enrichment. Multiple testing in the conventional analyses was
controlled with the Benjamini-Hochberg procedure
(\citeproc{ref-BenjaminiHochberg1995}{Benjamini and Hochberg 1995}).
We treat BH-adjusted frequentist results as the primary evidence for
screen-level discovery and use Bayesian estimates as complementary
stabilized summaries, especially for sparse labels where maximum
likelihood estimates can be unstable or prespecified support filters
exclude labels from the primary adjusted screen.

For the AGP self-reported IBD follow-up, we repeated the discovery-stage
analysis after excluding non-IBD controls with the other AGP condition
strings analyzed in this study (IBS, autoimmune disease, acid reflux, or
seasonal allergies). Selected AGP IBD labels were also compared under
the absorb and pure-policy hierarchies to check whether the main
discovery-stage lineage summaries depended on absorption of
low-frequency dominance patterns (Supplementary Table S9). The
primary external analysis then froze the AGP-derived absorb
dCST-hierarchy, aligned each external cohort to the AGP retained-feature
dictionary, and mapped external samples into the AGP-derived label
structure. This direct AGP-derived label transfer was the main test of
portable disease association. Rebuilt-cohort validation, in which each
external matrix was filtered and analyzed independently with the same
absorb workflow used for the discovery cohort, served as a secondary
comparison that asked whether a related dominance structure would be
rediscovered without enforcing the AGP labels themselves. The completed
validation cohorts included HMP2 / IBDMDB, Halfvarson 2017, Gevers 2014,
PRJEB84421, and Jacobs 2023 IBS 250bp; we emphasize the
clinically annotated IBD cohorts because they provide the most
admissible disease interpretation. Where external metadata allowed it,
the main validation contrasts separated pooled IBD, Crohn disease, and
ulcerative colitis. For cohorts with repeated subjects, we repeated
leading validation tests after retaining the first detected sample per
subject, and for direct AGP-derived label transfer we summarized
case/control mapping rates by depth (Supplementary Tables S12 and S13).
We also summarized repeated-sample sensitivity with a
bootstrap-resample-one-per-subject consensus: for each leading rebuilt
and direct-transfer comparison, one sample per subject was drawn
uniformly at random across \(B = 1000\) iterations, and we recorded the
consensus rate (\(\Pr_{\text{boot}}(q < 0.05)\)), the median adjusted
q-value, and the empirical 95\% percentile interval of the
Jeffreys-corrected odds ratio across iterations. This uses information
from every collected time point rather than privileging the first
available sample, and it directly quantifies sensitivity to which sample
is selected for each subject. It does not, however, increase the number
of unique control subjects available in any cohort, so for Halfvarson
the nine-subject pooled-control arm remains the binding constraint on
subject-level inference under any sensitivity strategy.
For interpretive consistency in the main text, we use a pragmatic
bootstrap-recurrence scale in which values at or above 50\% are treated
as the working threshold for subject-level stability, values between
25\% and 50\% as suggestive but not stable, and values below 25\% as
weak with respect to repeated-sample selection.
This bootstrap summary is reported alongside the first-sample analysis in
Supplementary Table S12. We applied the same subject-aware logic to the
Halfvarson within-disease follow-up: for UC extent and Crohn location,
case/control status was fixed at the subject level and one-sample-per-subject
sensitivity was summarized for the sample-level top labels, whereas for the
Crohn calprotectin split the primary analysis remained visit-level and the
qualifier columns re-evaluated high-versus-low status from the selected sample
in each sensitivity run (Supplementary Table S13D). We also used a
threshold sweep at 100, 150, 200, and 250~\textmu g/g to check whether
any thresholded signal was confined to the higher end of the clinically
plausible range (Supplementary Table S13I and Supplementary Figure~S2).
For the Halfvarson within-disease follow-up, UC extent followed the
Montreal E1/E2/E3 schema and Crohn location followed the Montreal
L1/L2/L3/L4 schema; in the binary location contrast reported here,
ileal-involved Crohn combined L1/L1+L4 and L3/L3+L4 while colonic-only
Crohn corresponded to L2
(\citeproc{ref-Satsangi2006_Montreal}{Satsangi et al. 2006}). The
Crohn calprotectin sensitivity split used 250~\textmu g/g as a
conservative high-inflammatory-burden threshold rather than as a
remission cut-off, so it should be read as a contrast between clearly
elevated and lower calprotectin visits rather than as a formal activity
definition (\citeproc{ref-Walsham2016_calprotectin}{Walsham and
Sherwood 2016}). A small supplemental threshold sweep at
100, 150, 200, and 250~\textmu g/g was used to check whether any
thresholded signal was confined to the higher end of the clinically
plausible range.
These three within-disease analyses were exploratory and were corrected
only within each comparison's label panel, not across one another.
For Gevers 2014, we additionally joined the analyzed stool subset to the
public ENA sample-attribute tables to recover explicit per-sample flags
for antibiotics, mesalamine, steroids, and biologics. Because no
analyzed Gevers stool sample was flagged biologics-positive, the
exploratory medication follow-up was restricted to antibiotics,
mesalamine, and steroids. We then asked two narrower questions using
the existing rebuilt and direct-transfer dCST assignments: whether the
Crohn-disease-versus-healthy comparison remained null after restricting
to medication-negative samples, and whether medication-positive Crohn
samples differed from medication-negative Crohn samples when status was
known. These Gevers analyses are stratified follow-up screens rather
than multivariable medication-adjusted models, and they are summarized
in Supplementary Table S13E.
The full comparison summary is given in Supplementary Tables S11A to
S11D. All
manuscript figures, tables, and analysis scripts were generated from
workflows built around the \texttt{linf} R package, with project-specific
runners maintained in the companion \texttt{gut\_microbiome} repository.

\section{Discussion}\label{general-discussion}

This study separates structure learning from disease inference. AGP was
most useful here as a large gut ecological cohort in which a dominant
taxon hierarchy could be learned without depending on a fitted
clustering model. The main disease-facing interpretation, however, comes
from transferring the AGP-derived dCST-hierarchy into external cohorts
with stronger IBD diagnosis metadata. Within the exploratory AGP screen,
self-reported IBD identified a multistate set of recurrent rank
contexts that motivated follow-up, but the paper's central claim does not depend on
treating AGP as a clinical IBD cohort.

The strongest current support for portability comes from Halfvarson
2017, where labels from the AGP-derived dCST-hierarchy remained
informative for pooled IBD, Crohn disease, and ulcerative colitis in
stool samples at the sample level, but where the
one-sample-per-subject sensitivity analysis showed that the direct
AGP-transfer disease-versus-control contrasts attenuated above
\(q = 0.05\) while rebuilt ulcerative colitis remained the clearest
subject-aware residual pattern by bootstrap recurrence rather than as a
second BH-significant subject-level finding. HMP2 /
IBDMDB and Gevers 2014 then helped define portability boundaries: the
current mucosal HMP2 slice mapped only at depth 1 under the canonical
shared-taxonomy AGP reference, and Gevers showed good hierarchy mapping
without corrected label-level transfer in the current pediatric
new-onset setting. Taken together, these cohorts
argue that dCSTs can represent known heterogeneous IBD dysbiosis in a
portable way, but that portability is not universal and depends on the
ecological and clinical setting.

The new within-disease Halfvarson follow-up sharpens that point. The
rebuilt-mode Crohn-location result indicates that the dCST framework can
recover additional clinically meaningful structure inside Crohn disease,
but the asymmetry between rebuilt and AGP-transferred modes is itself
informative. The leading rebuilt depth-2
\emph{Bacteroides~dorei}/\emph{Faecalibacterium~prausnitzii} label
approached our working subject-level stability threshold, whereas the
corresponding AGP-frozen labels lost support once one-sample-per-subject
sensitivity was imposed. The most honest interpretation is not that
within-disease structure is absent, but that the AGP-frozen vocabulary
is better calibrated to disease-versus-control summaries than to a more
specific ileal-versus-colonic Crohn distinction. In other words, dCSTs
appear well suited to portable IBD dysbiosis representation, but a
fixed AGP-derived label dictionary need not capture every clinically
meaningful subtype boundary without rebuilding or re-anchoring the
hierarchy in a subtype-enriched cohort.

A note on the current canonical AGP IBD discovery pattern is worth
making explicit. It is no longer a single broad
\taxon{Bacteroides}-dominant story. Instead, the leading AGP labels are
multistate and context dependent: enriched
\taxon{[Eubacterium]~eligens~group~eligens} and \taxon{Alistipes}
states, recurring deeper \taxon{Bacteroides~dorei}-linked contexts, and
depletion of \taxon{Segatella}. Because dCST assignment depends on
within-sample rank order rather than absolute abundance, those labels
should be read as ordered retained-feature ecological contexts rather
than as single-taxon abundance thresholds. The retained-feature profiles
in Supplementary Table S7 are therefore best interpreted as evidence
that IBD in AGP is associated with redistribution among a small number
of recurrent ranked contexts, not with one universal bloom or collapse
of a single parent taxon.

The Halfvarson Crohn-location result fits naturally into the same
ecological reading. Ileal-involved Crohn disease was defined by
depletion of \taxon{Faecalibacterium}-linked states together with
\taxon{Escherichia-Shigella}/enterobacterial enrichment relative to
colonic-only Crohn disease, which is precisely the kind of ileal-CD
microbial pattern that motivated the classical adherent-invasive
\emph{E.~coli} literature
(\citeproc{ref-DarfeuilleMichaud2004_AIEC_ileal_CD}{Darfeuille-Michaud
et al. 2004}). We therefore view the rebuilt Crohn-location signal not
as an arbitrary subtype split but as recovery of an established
ileal-Crohn ecological signature inside the broader dCST framework.

The Gevers portability boundary deserves a more specific
interpretation. Several non-exclusive factors plausibly contribute to
why Gevers samples mapped well into the AGP-derived hierarchy but did
not retain corrected label-level transfer. First, the Gevers cohort is
pediatric and treatment-naive, whereas AGP is overwhelmingly adult and
treatment-mixed: the gut microbiome matures into adult-like
configurations only over the first three to five years of life and
continues to drift through adolescence, so an AGP-derived hierarchy may
be calibrated to a successional context that pediatric new-onset
disease has not yet entered. Second, treatment-naive disease has not
been perturbed by 5-aminosalicylates, immunomodulators, biologics, or
repeated antibiotic courses, all of which contribute to the dysbiotic
signal observed in adult IBD cohorts; the AGP-derived dictionary may
therefore over-represent treatment-shaped community structure. Third,
the Gevers stool control arm in the AGP-overlap-filtered slice is small
(33 samples from 32 subjects), which limits power for any per-state
contrast after BH correction even when case-side enrichment is
directionally present. Exploratory medication-stratified follow-up in
the same Gevers stool slice also suggests that medication alone is not a
sufficient explanation for the portability boundary: restricting to
explicit antibiotic-negative, mesalamine-negative, or steroid-negative
samples still did not recover corrected Crohn-versus-healthy transfer,
whereas antibiotic-positive Crohn samples showed their own transferred
\taxon{Bacteroides} depletion and
\taxon{Escherichia-Shigella}\dcstsep\taxon{Bifidobacterium}
enrichment pattern. Taken together, the most clinically relevant
reading of the Gevers result is that dCSTs as currently constructed are
calibrated primarily to adult stool ecologies and that pediatric,
treatment-naive, or medication-perturbed applications will likely
require either a separate calibration cohort or explicit cross-cohort
harmonization rather than direct AGP-label transfer.

The contribution of this application is therefore representational and
analytical rather than biomarker discovery. The deterministic dCST label
provides a rank-based sample-level state, the absorb hierarchy preserves
named deeper dominance-lineages instead of sending a growing fraction of
samples into a residual tail, and the Bayesian layer keeps sparse-label
estimates finite while leaving BH-adjusted frequentist results as the
primary screen-level discovery framework. From a systems perspective,
the main value of the framework is that it creates a stable, reusable
state vocabulary that can be transported across cohorts without
re-fitting a clustering model at each new study. The AGP self-reported
routed-to-supplement AGP follow-up is best read as exploratory
prioritization and calibration
rather than as stand-alone clinical evidence. The same is true for the
broader non-IBD condition screens, which are retained in the supplement
rather than in the main disease narrative.

That representational framing motivates several near- and medium-term
clinical applications that go beyond the present case-control transfer
analyses, all of which require prospective validation before any
clinical use. (i) \emph{Cross-cohort meta-analysis standardization}:
because dCST labels are deterministic and pipeline-agnostic, reporting
cohort composition in dCST terms (for example, the proportions of
\taxon{Bacteroides~dorei}-, \taxon{Segatella}-, or
\taxon{Faecalibacterium~prausnitzii}-linked states in an enrolled
cohort) gives reviewers and meta-analysts a common vocabulary that does
not require re-running clustering on harmonized counts. (ii)
\emph{Trial stratification}: pre-treatment dCST could be used as a
stratification or pre-specified subgroup variable in
microbiome-modulating intervention trials such as fecal microbiota
transplantation, defined consortia, prebiotics, or exclusive enteral
nutrition, since rank-based labels survive minor pipeline differences
between trial sites. (iii) \emph{Longitudinal response monitoring}:
serial stool dCST transitions in a single patient \(-\) for example,
exit from a depleted-\taxon{Segatella} or
\taxon{Faecalibacterium}-linked state during induction therapy, or
drift toward an Enterococcus- or enterobacterial-associated state
\(-\) provide a transparent ecological response variable that does not
depend on a fitted cluster model. (iv) \emph{FMT engraftment
operationalization}: pre-vs-post-FMT dCST transitions provide a
transparent, model-free operationalization of ecological engraftment,
which currently lacks a standard cross-study definition. (v)
\emph{Cohort enrichment for downstream omics}: when designing a
metabolomic or functional metagenomic follow-up study, enriching for
specific dCSTs can increase statistical power for mechanism-focused
hypotheses, for example enriching for \taxon{Faecalibacterium}-depleted
dCSTs in studies of short-chain-fatty-acid-producing capacity. None of
these use cases is supported by the present data on its own; each
should be tested in a prospectively designed cohort before being used
to inform decisions about individual patients.

Compared with established cohort-summary frameworks, dCSTs are
intentionally conservative. PAM-style enterotypes
(\citeproc{ref-Arumugam2011_enterotypes}{Arumugam et al. 2011};
\citeproc{ref-Costea2018_enterotypes}{Costea et al. 2018}), Dirichlet
multinomial mixtures, and dysbiosis indices such as the disease-axis
ratios proposed by \citeproc{ref-Gevers2014_CD}{Gevers et al. (2014)}
all share with dCSTs the goal of compressing a high-dimensional gut
abundance profile into an interpretable summary, but each fits a model
at the cohort level. dCSTs differ in that no model is fit: a label is
determined by within-sample rank order alone, so a
\taxon{Bacteroides}/\taxon{Faecalibacterium} dCST in AGP and the same
label assigned in Halfvarson have identical semantics by construction.
Deterministic rank-based summaries do, however, discard quantitative
information that mixture-model and dysbiosis-index approaches preserve,
and they are not designed to capture continuous within-state variation.
We therefore view dCSTs as complementary to abundance-modeling
approaches rather than as a replacement: dCSTs are best suited to
labeling-and-portability questions, while quantitative dysbiosis indices
remain the natural tool for graded ecological severity scoring.

Several limitations remain important. AGP condition fields are
participant-reported and cross-sectional, so residual confounding,
medication effects, antibiotic exposure, and reverse causation remain
plausible even though the leading AGP IBD dominance-lineages were
robust to the coarse antibiotic-status checks available here. External
validation supports IBD-focused dominance-pattern transfer more
strongly than universal label portability: Halfvarson is the clearest
positive stool-based example at the sample level, with rebuilt
ulcerative colitis the clearest subject-level disease-versus-control
signal; HMP2 provides a clinically rich but ecologically non-identical
mucosal boundary under the canonical shared-taxonomy reference; and
Gevers mainly provides a Crohn-focused pediatric portability boundary.

Several IBD-specific covariates were not modeled in the present
analysis. Disease activity (active vs.~quiescent), Crohn disease
location (ileal, colonic, ileocolonic), ulcerative colitis extent
(proctitis, left-sided, pancolitis), and medication exposure
(5-aminosalicylates, immunomodulators, biologics, recent
corticosteroids) all shape the gut microbiome and are at least
partially available in the validation cohorts we used. We did not
stratify by these axes for two reasons: the AGP discovery cohort lacks
them entirely, and the per-stratum sample sizes available within
Halfvarson and HMP2 would not have supported subtype-by-stratum testing
without further loss of correction-significant signal. We therefore
used these axes selectively: we include an exploratory
medication-stratified follow-up for Gevers and within-disease
Halfvarson follow-up for UC extent, Crohn location, and Crohn
calprotectin, but the pooled and CD/UC-resolved transfer results
reported here should still be read as largely marginal across activity
and treatment status. Broader activity-stratified and
medication-stratified follow-up of the same external cohorts remains a
natural next step, with HMP2 subtype/activity follow-up in a
stool-equivalent slice being the most obvious extension. A more
specific residual source of heterogeneity is
PSC-IBD overlap within Halfvarson: PSC status was not explicitly
resolved in the harmonized validation slice used here, so it could not
be stratified and may contribute residual within-IBD heterogeneity
independent of the bootstrap sensitivity checks.

The dCST labels are also retained-feature labels constructed after
prevalence filtering, and some low-resolution or potential non-gut
labels appear in the full hierarchy; those states are retained for
transparent accounting but require caution as biological signals.
Several of the species-level retained-feature labels that appear in
deeper dominance-lineages \(-\) for example \taxon{Bacteroides dorei},
\taxon{Bacteroides vulgatus}, and \taxon{Faecalibacterium prausnitzii}
\(-\) are at or near the limit of what V3-V4 16S rRNA gene fragments
can reliably resolve under a SILVA assignment. We retain these labels
because they are the deepest informative annotations supplied by the
upstream pipeline, but they should not be read as strain-level
identifications. Where two labels in the same dominance-lineage refer
to closely related \taxon{Bacteroides} species, the relevant biological
inference is dominance by the genus \taxon{Bacteroides} with a
particular ecological co-occurrence pattern, rather than presence of a
specific named species.

A broader replication strategy would also benefit from a more
informative subject-level sensitivity analysis. The present
one-sample-per-subject sensitivity tests retained the first available
sample per participant in the repeated-sample cohorts, and
Supplementary Table S12 now complements that summary with a
subject-level resampling consensus in which one sample per subject is
drawn at random across many iterations and the consensus rate, median
adjusted q-value, and empirical odds-ratio interval are reported. This
addresses pseudoreplication more thoroughly than a single first-sample
selection because it uses information from every collected time point,
but it cannot increase the number of unique subjects in the Halfvarson
healthy-control arm, which contains only nine individuals. That
cohort-level constraint is independent of the resampling choice and
remains the binding limitation on subject-level inference for
Halfvarson under any analytic strategy. The current evidence
nevertheless supports absorb-policy dCSTs as a biologically
interpretable analysis layer and motivates broader testing of the
AGP-derived dCST-hierarchy in independent, clinically curated cohorts
with larger control arms and resolved disease-activity and medication
metadata.

\section{Conclusion}\label{conclusion}

Taken together, these results support dCSTs as a compact, interpretable
way to summarize established heterogeneous IBD-associated gut dysbiosis
as sample-level dominance structure. In this manuscript, AGP is most
useful as a structure-learning cohort, whereas the main disease-facing
support comes from transferring labels from the AGP-derived
dCST-hierarchy into clinically annotated external IBD cohorts, with the
strongest current sample-level evidence in Halfvarson, the clearest
subject-level disease-versus-control support in rebuilt ulcerative
colitis, a rebuilt-mode Crohn location follow-up that approaches the
working bootstrap stability threshold without matching AGP-transfer
support, and HMP2/Gevers serving mainly as portability boundaries under
the canonical shared-taxonomy reference. More broadly,
the framework is a portable analysis layer that should be most useful
when paired with the next round of clinically curated, functional, or
multi-omics follow-up rather than treated as an end point in itself.
