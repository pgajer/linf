\section{Abstract}\label{abstract}

Gut microbiome community typing is often cluster based, which makes the
resulting labels sensitive to modeling choices and harder to interpret
sample by sample. We evaluated dominant-community state types (DCSTs),
a deterministic rank-based alternative, in 30,290 quality-controlled
American Gut Project stool samples drawn from a 34,711-sample SILVA
species-level table. At depth 1, the cohort resolved into 40 common
DCSTs plus a rare-dominant tail; at depth 2, subtype structure exposed
substantial heterogeneity within broad parent states. Across 452
covariate-adjusted depth-1 tests, 28 associations were significant
after Benjamini-Hochberg correction and 23 remained significant after a
contamination-aware sensitivity analysis; depth 2 contributed 23
additional adjusted-significant associations. The clearest completed
signals concentrated in inflammatory and gastrointestinal phenotypes,
especially IBD. Dominant-genus assignments were largely stable across
SILVA and GG2 (27,739 / 30,290 overlapping samples; 91.6\%
harmonized-genus agreement), and four completed IBD-relevant external
cohorts supported structural or directional generalization rather than
uniform exact replication, with the strongest positive support in HMP2
and Halfvarson. Together, these results position DCSTs as a practical,
reproducible, and interpretable framework for large-scale microbiome
association screens while also showing that external validation remains
phenotype dependent rather than automatic.

\section{Importance}\label{importance}

Large microbiome studies need community-typing schemes that remain
interpretable after preprocessing choices, covariate adjustment, and
cross-cohort comparison. This study shows that a simple rank-based
dominant-state representation can recover structured ecological states
from a very large gut cohort without fitting global clusters, while also
making it easy to separate durable inflammatory signals from weaker or
more fragile associations. The strongest completed use case is
inflammatory bowel disease, where discovery, subtype refinement,
taxonomy concordance, and external validation point in the same
direction. The main contribution is therefore not a claim of causality,
but a reproducible workflow for turning large microbiome tables into
explicit sample-level states that can be screened, interpreted, and
tested in independent cohorts.

\textbf{Keywords:} gut microbiome; community typing; dominant-community
state types; American Gut Project; inflammatory bowel disease; external
validation

\section{Introduction}\label{introduction}

Microbiome community typing is useful when it compresses a
high-dimensional abundance profile into a smaller number of biologically
interpretable states. In practice, however, many typing schemes are
built around clustering. That can work well, but it also ties the final
labels to choices about transformations, distance metrics,
initialization, model class, and the number of clusters. Those choices
are not merely technical. They affect whether a reader can understand
what a given sample label means and whether two analyses can be compared
without a hidden dependence on tuning decisions.

This paper studies a simpler route. A dominant-community state type
(DCST) is defined by the within-sample ordering of taxa. At depth 1, the
dominant taxon determines the top-level state. At depth 2, the
second-ranked taxon refines that state into an ordered subtype. Because
the construction depends only on rank order, it is unchanged by
sample-wide rescaling and does not require a particular \(L_p\)
normalization. The resulting construction is deterministic,
hierarchical, and local to the sample: the label is read directly from
the ordered dominant coordinates rather than inferred from a fitted
global partition.

The gut is a particularly good stress test for this idea. In the vaginal
microbiome, strong dominance can be common and even canonical. In the
gut, strong dominance is less universal and often appears in dysbiotic
or otherwise unusual settings. That makes the gut harder to type
cleanly, but also more interesting. Literature on IBS, IBD, autoimmune
disease, and cardiometabolic conditions suggests that shifts in dominant
taxa or dominant taxon pairs can mark altered ecological states
(\citeproc{ref-Tap2017_IBS}{Tap et al. 2017};
\citeproc{ref-Pittayanon2019_IBS}{Pittayanon et al. 2019};
\citeproc{ref-LloydPrice2019_IBD}{Lloyd-Price et al. 2019};
\citeproc{ref-Gevers2014_CD}{Gevers et al. 2014};
\citeproc{ref-Scher2013_Prevotella_RA}{Scher et al. 2013};
\citeproc{ref-Vatanen2018_T1D}{Vatanen et al. 2018};
\citeproc{ref-Wang2011_TMAO}{Z. Wang et al. 2011};
\citeproc{ref-Tang2013_TMAO}{Tang et al. 2013}).

Our aim is not to argue that dominant taxa alone explain disease
biology. The claim is narrower and more operational. First, recurring
dominance patterns can be identified deterministically across a cohort
of this size without fitting clusters. Second, those labels retain
enough ecological meaning to support phenotype-resolved screening rather
than only one pooled disease-versus-not-disease contrast. Third, the
strongest signals can be stress-tested across covariate adjustment,
contaminant-aware filtering, taxonomy change, and external validation.
That combination makes DCSTs attractive as a systems-level analysis
layer even when mechanistic follow-up remains incomplete.

A DCST is defined by the ordered dominant taxa in a sample. At depth 1,
only the largest component matters. At depth 2, the second-largest
component refines the parent class. Because only the within-sample
ordering matters, the same DCST is obtained from read counts,
proportions, or any sample-wide \(L_p\) normalization. How low-frequency
dominant patterns are handled is a separate modeling choice. In the
\texttt{rare} policy used here, samples that do not meet the prevalence
threshold for a named dominant type are grouped into
\texttt{RARE\_DOMINANT}.

\section{Materials and Methods}\label{methods}

\subsection{Cohort and
preprocessing}\label{cohort-and-preprocessing}

The discovery analysis used AGP gut samples from the PRIME-derived SILVA
species-level abundance table (\citeproc{ref-McDonald2018_AGP}{McDonald
et al. 2018}; \citeproc{ref-Quast2013_SILVA}{Quast et al. 2013}). The
starting table contained 34,711 samples, of which 30,290 passed
quality-control filters and were retained for analysis. The retained
matrix contained 274 taxa. Age was available for 27,372 samples
(90.4\%), sex for 25,598 (84.5\%), and BMI for 26,541 (87.6\%),
producing 22,632 complete cases for adjusted modeling.

\subsection{DCST construction}\label{dcst-construction}

A depth-1 DCST was defined by the dominant taxon. A depth-2 DCST was
defined by the ordered pair consisting of the dominant and second-ranked
taxa. This construction depends only on within-sample rank order, so the
resulting label is unchanged by moving between raw counts, relative
abundances, or any sample-wide \(L_p\) normalization. To keep the
resulting state space interpretable, low-frequency dominant patterns
were handled by a separate policy choice. In the software this
corresponds to \texttt{low.freq.policy}. Under
\texttt{low.freq.policy\ =\ "rare"} (used here), only dominant patterns
meeting a minimum prevalence threshold \(n_0\) were promoted to named
DCSTs, and the rest were grouped into \texttt{RARE\_DOMINANT}. This
preserves the purity of the named DCSTs because rare cells are not
absorbed into larger ones. The alternative
\texttt{low.freq.policy\ =\ "absorb"} merges low-frequency cells into
larger named states, which can yield a coarser partition but disturbs
the original dominant structure of those named DCSTs.

\subsection{Association analysis}\label{association-analysis}

We first performed univariate screening with Fisher's exact test. We
then fit adjusted logistic models for disease indicators using the DCST
label together with available covariates. For a mathematically oriented
reader, the odds ratio may be read as follows: an odds ratio above 1
means the DCST is enriched in the given phenotype relative to the
reference group, while an odds ratio below 1 means depletion. Multiple
testing was handled by the Benjamini-Hochberg procedure
(\citeproc{ref-BenjaminiHochberg1995}{Benjamini and Hochberg 1995}), and
q-values below 0.05 were treated as statistically notable rather than as
proof of mechanism.

\subsection{Sensitivity analysis and taxonomy
comparison}\label{sensitivity-analysis-and-taxonomy-comparison}

Because some nominal gut signals can be driven by oral- or
skin-associated contaminants, we repeated the adjusted analysis after
excluding samples dominated by a prespecified contaminant set. We also
ran a parallel dominant-type analysis under GG2 taxonomy
(\citeproc{ref-McDonald2024_GG2}{McDonald et al. 2024}) and compared
dominant-genus assignments between SILVA and GG2 on the overlapping
sample set.

\subsection{External validation}\label{external-validation}

The validation program now has two layers. The manuscript's main-text
validation layer uses four completed IBD-relevant cohorts: HMP2 / IBDMDB
(\texttt{PRJNA398089}), PRJEB84421, Halfvarson 2017, and Gevers 2014.
HMP2 provides clinically grounded inflammatory bowel disease phenotypes,
but in the currently available local metadata it is dominated by mucosal
gut sites and repeated measures. PRJEB84421 provides a stool-based
inflammatory complement, but is smaller, pediatric, and phenotype-mixed.
Halfvarson 2017 and Gevers 2014 were reprocessed from raw external 16S
data with DADA2 and then harmonized into the same validation framework.
A broader completed-validation layer also includes non-IBD cohorts, but
those are used mainly to evaluate how broadly the pipeline's positive,
null, and near-null outcomes generalize beyond the IBD branch.

\subsection{Reproducibility and manuscript reporting
policy}\label{reproducibility-and-manuscript-reporting-policy}

All manuscript-facing tables, figures, and text updates were generated
from version-controlled assets in the public \texttt{linf} repository,
with cohort-scale processing and validation outputs maintained in the
companion gut-microbiome working tree. External cohorts were interpreted
in the main text only after quality control, phenotype harmonization,
and a complete association pass under the same DCST workflow. Cohorts
still in processing are not treated as positive or negative evidence in
this draft; instead, the manuscript carries explicit submission-stage
placeholders so that additional external results can be added without
blurring completed evidence and pending work.

\section{Results}\label{cohort-overview}

\subsection{Discovery cohort landscape}\label{shared-agp-overview}

The Phase 1 discovery analysis used 30,290 quality-controlled AGP gut
samples. At depth 1 with \(n_0 = 50\), the cohort yielded 40 named DCSTs
plus \texttt{RARE\_DOMINANT} (1,162 samples). The largest types were
\texttt{Bacteroides} (10,358 samples), \texttt{Escherichia-Shigella}
(6,857), \texttt{Prevotella\_9} (1,870), \texttt{Enterobacteriaceae}
(1,564), and \texttt{Faecalibacterium} (1,320). In other words, the gut
landscape is neither one universal dominant state nor an uninterpretable
spray of rare labels.

Across the full screen, 59 of 492 univariate Fisher tests were
significant after correction. The covariate-adjusted depth-1 pass
yielded 28 significant associations across 452 tests, and 23 of those
remained significant in the contamination-aware sensitivity analysis.
Depth 2 added 23 adjusted-significant subtype associations across 1,340
tests. This common overview is useful because the disease-wise sections
below should be read against this shared statistical background rather
than as isolated anecdotes. The twelve most common depth-1 states
already cover 87.6\% of the retained cohort, so the dominant-state
picture is concentrated rather than diffusely fragmented (Table 1).

\subsection{Taxonomy concordance: SILVA versus
GreenGenes2}\label{taxonomy-concordance-silva-versus-greengenes2}

An independent dominant-type pass under GG2 identified 59 named depth-1
DCSTs plus \texttt{RARE\_DOMINANT}. On the overlapping 30,290 samples,
the exact dominant-genus agreement between SILVA and GG2 was 27,739 /
30,290 (91.6\%). This does not mean that the taxonomies are
interchangeable. It does mean that the main dominant-typing picture is
fairly stable across reference databases, even though some clinically
relevant groups, especially \texttt{Escherichia-Shigella}, expose
genuine naming and resolution differences. The new concordance figure

\section{Phenotype-Resolved Results}\label{disease-wise-results-and-interpretation}

The subsections in this section follow a common pattern. Each begins
with a brief literature frame, then summarizes the AGP signal across
SILVA, GG2, depth 2, and sensitivity analysis, and finally interprets
the result at a level that matches the strength of the evidence. This
repeated structure is intentional: it lets the reader compare diseases
without losing track of which claims are strong, which are tentative,
and which are mainly useful for context. Several AGP phenotypes,
especially autoimmune disease, seasonal allergies, and lung disease, are
best understood as discovery-screen labels rather than harmonized
clinical diagnoses, so their discussion leans more on ecological
interpretation than on disease-specific inference.

For submission, the main biological emphasis should stay on the clearest
completed signals: IBD first, then IBS, seasonal allergies, CDI, and
kidney disease. The remaining phenotypes are still useful because they
show where the framework becomes hypothesis generating rather than
stable, but they should not be presented as coequal headline findings.

\subsection{Irritable bowel syndrome
(IBS)}\label{irritable-bowel-syndrome-ibs}


IBS is a functional gastrointestinal disorder in which the gut
microbiota has been implicated through immune signaling, short-chain
fatty acid production, and the microbiota-gut-brain axis
(\citeproc{ref-Pittayanon2019_IBS}{Pittayanon et al. 2019};
\citeproc{ref-Dinan2015_IBS}{Dinan and Cryan 2015};
\citeproc{ref-Cryan2012_gutbrain}{Cryan and Dinan 2012}). Because many
IBS signatures are subtle rather than catastrophically dysbiotic, it is
a useful test of whether dominant-state labels retain signal in a noisy
population cohort.


In SILVA, the strongest adjusted depth-1 IBS association was enrichment
of \texttt{Escherichia-Shigella} (adjusted OR 1.23, q = 6.8e-04),
accompanied by protective associations for \texttt{Faecalibacterium}
(adjusted OR 0.64), \texttt{Staphylococcus} (adjusted OR 0.46), and
\texttt{Streptococcus} (adjusted OR 0.61). GG2 showed a qualitatively
similar picture, with an enriched unresolved enterobacterial signal and
depleted \texttt{Staphylococcus} and \texttt{Streptococcus} states. At
depth 2, additional IBS-related subtype signal appeared in
\texttt{Escherichia-Shigella}- and \texttt{Bacteroides}-centered
subtypes. Sensitivity analysis retained \texttt{Faecalibacterium},
\texttt{Escherichia-Shigella}, and \texttt{Prevotella\_9}, suggesting
that the main IBS pattern is not driven entirely by putative oral
contaminants.


These IBS findings are consistent with literature linking
Enterobacteriaceae expansion and loss of anti-inflammatory commensals to
symptom burden (\citeproc{ref-Pittayanon2019_IBS}{Pittayanon et al.
2019}; \citeproc{ref-Tap2017_IBS}{Tap et al. 2017}). The
\texttt{Faecalibacterium} signal is particularly interpretable for a
mechanistic reader, because it points toward loss of butyrate-associated
ecological stability rather than toward one IBS-specific pathogen. The
oral-associated taxa should still be treated cautiously, and the overall
effect sizes are modest compared with IBD.

\subsection{Inflammatory bowel disease
(IBD)}\label{inflammatory-bowel-disease-ibd}


IBD provides the most clinically grounded inflammatory phenotype in the
AGP screen. Landmark studies report reduced diversity, expansion of
Proteobacteria, and depletion of butyrate-associated commensals,
especially \emph{Faecalibacterium prausnitzii}
(\citeproc{ref-Frank2007_IBD}{Frank et al. 2007};
\citeproc{ref-Gevers2014_CD}{Gevers et al. 2014};
\citeproc{ref-LloydPrice2019_IBD}{Lloyd-Price et al. 2019};
\citeproc{ref-Sokol2008_Fprausnitzii}{Sokol et al. 2008};
\citeproc{ref-Manichanh2006_CD}{Manichanh et al. 2006}).


IBD produced the strongest adjusted depth-1 signal in the entire Phase 1
scan: \texttt{Morganella} was highly enriched (adjusted OR 16.94, q =
4.0e-15). Additional adjusted SILVA signals included enrichment of
\texttt{Bacteroides}, \texttt{Lachnospiraceae},
\texttt{Bifidobacterium}, \texttt{Enterobacterales}, and
\texttt{Proteus}, along with depletion of \texttt{Prevotella\_9}. GG2
showed concordant inflammatory structure, again highlighting
\texttt{Morganella} and related enterobacterial signals. Depth 2 was
even more striking: a \texttt{Bacteroides} subtype with a
\texttt{Lachnospiraceae} second-ranked component reached adjusted OR
3.54 with q = 1.6e-37, and multiple IBD-associated subtypes appeared
within the broad \texttt{Bacteroides} parent class. Eight IBD
associations survived sensitivity analysis.


IBD is the clearest case here where depth 2 matters both scientifically
and mathematically. The established IBD literature emphasizes depletion
of obligate anaerobes and butyrate-associated commensals, especially
\emph{Faecalibacterium prausnitzii}, together with expansion of
inflammatory facultative taxa (\citeproc{ref-Frank2007_IBD}{Frank et al.
2007}; \citeproc{ref-Gevers2014_CD}{Gevers et al. 2014};
\citeproc{ref-LloydPrice2019_IBD}{Lloyd-Price et al. 2019};
\citeproc{ref-Sokol2008_Fprausnitzii}{Sokol et al. 2008};
\citeproc{ref-Sartor2008_IBD}{Sartor 2008}). The present DCST results
fit that picture in a compressed rank-based language: a rare
\texttt{Morganella} state marks severe enterobacterial dysbiosis,
whereas the broad \texttt{Bacteroides} parent resolves into subtypes
with materially different odds ratios. That matters for interpretation
because the enriched depth-1 \texttt{Bacteroides} label is not the
opposite of the classic \texttt{Faecalibacterium}-depletion story;
rather, it partly reflects loss of protective second-rank partners
inside Bacteroides-dominant samples.

External validation sharpened this IBD interpretation more than for any
other phenotype in the paper. HMP2 / IBDMDB
(\texttt{PRJNA398089}) contributed the clinically grounded inflammatory
validation arm. After phenotype filtering, the local cohort contained
166 samples: 45 healthy controls, 81 Crohn's disease samples, and 40
ulcerative colitis samples. The caveat is ecological: in the current
local metadata, the retained set is dominated by rectal, ileal, and
colonic sites and includes repeated measures. At depth 1, the HMP2
landscape was too sparse to yield strong multiple-testing-corrected
disease signal. At depth 2, however, a
\texttt{Bacteroides\_\_Faecalibacterium} subtype was depleted in
\texttt{IBD\_vs\_Healthy} (OR = 0.391, q = 0.016),
\texttt{UC\_vs\_Healthy} (OR = 0.318, q = 0.032), and
\texttt{Crohn\_vs\_Healthy} (OR = 0.429, q = 0.041). PRJEB84421 supplied
the stool-based complement. It retained 73 samples split across 20
healthy controls, 24 Crohn's disease samples, and 29 pediatric
orofacial granulomatosis samples. This cohort is a better ecological
match to AGP stool than HMP2, but it is smaller and phenotype mixed; its
strongest signals were therefore more modest and cohort specific, with
\texttt{RARE\_DOMINANT} enriched in \texttt{OFG\_vs\_Healthy} at depth 1
(OR = 4.93, q = 0.062) and in the combined inflammatory-versus-healthy
comparison at depth 2 (OR = 4.94, q = 0.084).

Halfvarson 2017 added the strongest currently completed external
positive validation. The final IBD-focused branch retained 533 samples
after QC and 379 taxa after filtering. Unlike HMP2, this cohort yielded
clear q-significant signal at both depths. At depth 1,
\texttt{Segatella} was depleted in \texttt{IBD\_vs\_Healthy} (OR =
0.136, q = 1.25e-09), \texttt{Crohn\_vs\_Healthy} (OR = 0.139, q =
2.73e-08), and \texttt{UC\_vs\_Healthy} (OR = 0.133, q = 3.40e-09). At
depth 2, the clearest replicated signal was depletion of a
\texttt{Segatella\_\_Faecalibacterium\ prausnitzii} subtype in
\texttt{IBD\_vs\_Healthy} (OR = 0.113, q = 6.37e-07) and
\texttt{UC\_vs\_Healthy} (OR = 0.169, q = 6.17e-05), with a separate
\texttt{RARE\_DOMINANT} expansion in \texttt{Crohn\_vs\_Healthy} (OR =
25.26, q = 7.23e-09). Gevers 2014 supplied a stool-only
Crohn-versus-healthy branch that retained 396 samples after QC and 332
taxa after filtering. This cohort is valuable because it completed the
same reprocessing and harmonization path as Halfvarson, but in the
current pass it did not yield q-significant depth-1 or depth-2 DCST
associations. Its strongest depth-1 signal was a
\texttt{Bacteroides\ vulgatus} state in \texttt{Crohn\_vs\_Healthy} (OR
= 0.465, q = 0.505), and its strongest depth-2 signal was a
\texttt{Faecalibacterium\ prausnitzii\_\_Bacteroides\ vulgatus} pair (OR
= 1.284, q = 1.000). That makes Gevers useful not as a positive
replicate, but as a completed weak/null control showing that the
validation pipeline does not produce strong inflammatory signal
automatically.

Taken together, the completed IBD-relevant validation cohorts support a
narrower but stronger claim than exact taxon-by-taxon replication. HMP2
and Halfvarson supply the clearest positive support: HMP2 at depth 2 in

\subsection{Autoimmune diseases}\label{autoimmune-diseases}


The AGP autoimmune phenotype is an umbrella label rather than a single
disease, so it should be interpreted more cautiously than IBD. Even so,
autoimmune disease is an important category because the literature
repeatedly links loss of immune-regulatory commensals and expansion of
pathobionts to altered Treg/Th17 balance
(\citeproc{ref-Scher2013_Prevotella_RA}{Scher et al. 2013};
\citeproc{ref-Atarashi2013_Clostridia_Treg}{Atarashi et al. 2013};
\citeproc{ref-DeLuca2019_autoimmune}{De Luca and Shoenfeld 2019};
\citeproc{ref-Vatanen2018_T1D}{Vatanen et al. 2018}).


In SILVA, autoimmune disease showed adjusted enrichment of
\texttt{Bacteroides} (adjusted OR 1.39, q = 2.1e-11) and
\texttt{Morganella} (adjusted OR 6.88, q = 1.9e-07), together with
depletion of \texttt{Prevotella\_9}, \texttt{Streptococcus},
\texttt{Faecalibacterium}, and \texttt{Staphylococcus}. GG2 recovered
the same broad pattern: enriched inflammatory or enterobacterial
structure and depleted \texttt{Prevotella} and \texttt{Streptococcus}
states. Depth 2 again sharpened the signal, especially within
\texttt{Bacteroides}-centered subtypes, where a major subtype reached
adjusted OR 2.25 with q = 1.2e-23. Four autoimmune associations survived
sensitivity analysis.


Because this AGP label pools multiple autoimmune conditions, the safest
reading is an inflammatory umbrella rather than a disease-specific
microbiome signature. The observed \texttt{Bacteroides} /
\texttt{Morganella} enrichment and \texttt{Faecalibacterium} /
\texttt{Prevotella\_9} depletion are broadly compatible with
disease-specific literature in RA and T1D, but the component mechanisms
differ: \texttt{Prevotella} expansion in RA is not the same biological
claim as early-life dysbiosis in T1D, even though both can be placed
within a broader Treg/Th17 or immune-education framework
(\citeproc{ref-Scher2013_Prevotella_RA}{Scher et al. 2013};
\citeproc{ref-Vatanen2018_T1D}{Vatanen et al. 2018};
\citeproc{ref-Atarashi2013_Clostridia_Treg}{Atarashi et al. 2013};
\citeproc{ref-DeLuca2019_autoimmune}{De Luca and Shoenfeld 2019}). The
parallel with IBD is therefore informative at the level of shared
inflammatory ecology, not at the level of one causal autoimmune taxon.

\subsection{Acid reflux / GERD}\label{acid-reflux-gerd}


GERD is increasingly discussed in microbiome terms rather than purely as
an acid-mediated disorder, with gram-negative-enriched states and loss
of gram-positive dominance implicated in esophageal disease
(\citeproc{ref-Deshpande2018_esophageal}{Deshpande et al. 2018};
\citeproc{ref-DeSouza2021_GERD}{D'Souza et al. 2021};
\citeproc{ref-Wang2024_GERD_MR}{K. Wang et al. 2024}).


In SILVA, acid reflux showed adjusted enrichment of \texttt{Bacteroides}
(adjusted OR 1.28, q = 6.9e-06) and depletion of \texttt{Streptococcus}
(adjusted OR 0.45, q = 4.2e-03) and \texttt{Staphylococcus} (adjusted OR
0.41, q = 1.1e-02). GG2 replicated the depletion of
\texttt{Streptococcus} and \texttt{Staphylococcus} together with a
depleted \texttt{Prevotella} signal. Depth 2 produced additional
\texttt{Parabacteroides}- and \texttt{Alistipes}-linked signals. After
contaminant-aware filtering, only the \texttt{Bacteroides} association
remained.


The reflux results are best interpreted as stool-side correlates of a
literature that remains centered on esophageal and oral compartments
rather than stool alone
(\citeproc{ref-Deshpande2018_esophageal}{Deshpande et al. 2018};
\citeproc{ref-DeSouza2021_GERD}{D'Souza et al. 2021}). In other words,
the disease-facing evidence is strongest for compartment-specific shifts
along the reflux-to-Barrett spectrum, whereas the AGP analysis sees only
a downstream gut-side view. The surviving \texttt{Bacteroides} signal
therefore supports a modest reflux-spectrum association rather than a
direct esophageal mechanism. By contrast, the oral-associated taxa are
more exposed to compartment choice, PPI effects, and oral-esophageal
carryover (\citeproc{ref-Wang2024_GERD_MR}{K. Wang et al. 2024}). The
clean analysis strengthens this section by narrowing the manuscript
claim to one conservative stool-side association instead of leaving a
mixed oral-plus-esophageal-plus-gut story unresolved.

\subsection{Cardiovascular disease}\label{cardiovascular-disease}


CVD is one of the most mechanistically developed microbiome literatures
because of the TMAO pathway, oral-gut cross-talk, and
inflammation-thrombosis links (\citeproc{ref-Wang2011_TMAO}{Z. Wang et
al. 2011}; \citeproc{ref-Tang2013_TMAO}{Tang et al. 2013};
\citeproc{ref-Jie2017_ASCVD}{Jie et al. 2017};
\citeproc{ref-Zhu2016_TMAO_thrombosis}{Zhu et al. 2016}).


The univariate CVD signals were strong, especially
\texttt{Pasteurellaceae}, \texttt{Staphylococcus}, and
\texttt{Neisseria}, but none survived covariate adjustment at depth 1.
GG2 showed analogous univariate enrichment of oral-associated or
aerotolerant taxa. Depth 2 nonetheless yielded a notable
\texttt{Agathobacter} association (adjusted OR 3.47, q = 2.6e-05). No
CVD signal survived the sensitivity analysis.


CVD illustrates why the covariate-adjusted pass matters. Without
adjustment, one could easily overstate the oral-associated signals.
After adjustment and contaminant-aware filtering, the evidence is much
weaker, so this section is best read as hypothesis-generating rather
than as a primary biological claim.

\subsection{Obesity}\label{obesity}


Obesity has a large and often contradictory microbiome literature,
ranging from energy-harvest hypotheses to gene-richness models and
\texttt{Akkermansia}-linked barrier effects
(\citeproc{ref-Turnbaugh2006_energy}{Turnbaugh et al. 2006};
\citeproc{ref-Ley2006_obesity}{Ley et al. 2006};
\citeproc{ref-LeChatelier2013_richness}{Le Chatelier et al. 2013};
\citeproc{ref-Everard2013_Akkermansia}{Everard et al. 2013};
\citeproc{ref-Walters2014_meta_obesity}{Walters, Xu, and Knight 2014}).


For obesity, the evidence remained at the univariate depth-1 level, led
by \texttt{Prevotella\_7} enrichment (OR 2.75) and weaker shifts in
\texttt{Escherichia-Shigella} and \texttt{Lactobacillus}. No depth-1
association survived covariate adjustment, GG2 mainly reproduced a broad
\texttt{Prevotella}-side signal, no depth-2 associations reached
significance, and nothing survived the sensitivity analysis.


These results suggest that dominant-state typing captures some
obesity-related structure, but that the signal is either confounded by
demographic variables or too diffuse to stabilize at the level of
top-taxon dominance. That is itself useful: it shows where the DCST
framework should remain modest rather than forcing every phenotype into
a clean association story.

\subsection{Seasonal allergies}\label{seasonal-allergies}


Seasonal allergy is another umbrella phenotype, but one with unusually
clear microbiome priors through the hygiene hypothesis and early-life
microbiota maturation literature
(\citeproc{ref-Strachan1989_hygiene}{Strachan 1989};
\citeproc{ref-Hua2016_AGP_allergy}{Hua et al. 2016};
\citeproc{ref-Hoskinson2023_allergy}{Hoskinson et al. 2023}).


In SILVA, seasonal allergies showed adjusted depletion of
\texttt{Prevotella\_9} (adjusted OR 0.67, q = 3.3e-08) and adjusted
enrichment of \texttt{Escherichia-Shigella} (adjusted OR 1.13, q =
5.1e-03) and \texttt{Enterobacteriaceae} (adjusted OR 1.21, q =
2.7e-02). GG2 showed the same broad pattern, especially strong
\texttt{Prevotella} depletion. Depth 2 added
\texttt{Bacteroides}-centered subtype signal, and all three adjusted
depth-1 associations survived sensitivity analysis.


Among the non-IBD outcomes, seasonal allergy is one of the cleaner
screen-level signals in the AGP scan. The protective \texttt{Prevotella}
pattern fits well with the broader allergy literature, which links
allergic phenotypes to lower richness, delayed microbiota maturation,
and weaker SCFA-associated immune education
(\citeproc{ref-Hua2016_AGP_allergy}{Hua et al. 2016};
\citeproc{ref-Hoskinson2023_allergy}{Hoskinson et al. 2023};
\citeproc{ref-Strachan1989_hygiene}{Strachan 1989}). The
Proteobacteria-side enrichment is likewise compatible with a more
dysbiotic, less tolerogenic gut ecosystem. Because the signal survives
sensitivity analysis and replicates qualitatively in GG2, it is one of
the stronger non-IBD findings. Even so, it is best read as an ecological
bridge between adult AGP allergy phenotypes and the early-life
maturation story, not as a definitive allergic-rhinitis-specific
signature.

\subsection{Diabetes}\label{diabetes}


Diabetes is mechanistically rich but clinically heterogeneous. The
literature links diabetes to reduced butyrate producers, endotoxemia,
medication effects, and altered fermentation ecology
(\citeproc{ref-Qin2012_T2D}{Qin et al. 2012};
\citeproc{ref-Karlsson2013_T2D_women}{Karlsson et al. 2013};
\citeproc{ref-Cani2007_endotoxemia}{Cani et al. 2007};
\citeproc{ref-Forslund2015_metformin}{Forslund et al. 2015};
\citeproc{ref-Takeuchi2023_carbohydrate}{Takeuchi et al. 2023}).


At depth 1, diabetes yielded only univariate depletion of
\texttt{Streptococcus} and \texttt{Staphylococcus}, with no
adjusted-significant depth-1 signal. GG2 again recovered only modest,
taxonomically heterogeneous univariate shifts. Depth 2, however,
produced a strong \texttt{Akkermansia}-associated subtype signal
(adjusted OR 4.88, q = 2.1e-04). No diabetes association survived the
sensitivity analysis.


The \texttt{Akkermansia} depth-2 result is biologically interesting
precisely because it is paradoxical if interpreted naively. Since
metformin can enrich \texttt{Akkermansia}, the cross-sectional signal
may encode medication rather than disease mechanism. The broader
diabetes literature is dominated by T2D, glucose-tolerance gradients,
and treatment confounding, so a clean disease-only signal is not
expected in a cross-sectional screen of this kind
(\citeproc{ref-Qin2012_T2D}{Qin et al. 2012};
\citeproc{ref-Karlsson2013_T2D_women}{Karlsson et al. 2013};
\citeproc{ref-Forslund2015_metformin}{Forslund et al. 2015}). The
present pattern therefore reads sensibly: no stable adjusted depth-1
signal, but one striking \texttt{Akkermansia}-associated depth-2 lead
that likely mixes disease status, treatment, and carbohydrate-metabolic
ecology (\citeproc{ref-Forslund2015_metformin}{Forslund et al. 2015};
\citeproc{ref-Takeuchi2023_carbohydrate}{Takeuchi et al. 2023};
\citeproc{ref-Cani2007_endotoxemia}{Cani et al. 2007}).

\subsection{\texorpdfstring{\emph{Clostridioides difficile}
infection
(CDI)}{Clostridioides difficile infection (CDI)}}\label{clostridioides-difficile-infection-cdi}


CDI is a dysbiosis-driven disease in which colonization resistance is
lost after antibiotic disruption, making it a natural target for
community-state analysis (\citeproc{ref-Schubert2014_CDI}{Schubert et
al. 2014}; \citeproc{ref-Seekatz2014_FMT}{Seekatz et al. 2014};
\citeproc{ref-Sehgal2021_CDI}{Sehgal and Khanna 2021}).


In SILVA, CDI showed adjusted enrichment of
\texttt{Escherichia-Shigella} (adjusted OR 1.48, q = 1.1e-02). GG2
highlighted related inflammatory or facultative-anaerobe structure,
including \texttt{Proteus}. Depth 2 added a strong
\texttt{Sutterella}-linked subtype signal (adjusted OR 9.85, q =
1.2e-05). After sensitivity analysis, \texttt{Escherichia-Shigella}
remained enriched and \texttt{Prevotella\_9} showed a protective
pattern.


CDI is one of the stronger non-umbrella disease sections because the
disease itself is defined by collapse and recovery of colonization
resistance (\citeproc{ref-Schubert2014_CDI}{Schubert et al. 2014};
\citeproc{ref-Seekatz2014_FMT}{Seekatz et al. 2014};
\citeproc{ref-Sehgal2021_CDI}{Sehgal and Khanna 2021}). The
enterobacterial enrichment is in line with recurrent-CDI dysbiosis, and
the protective \texttt{Prevotella\_9} direction is at least ecologically
compatible with restoration of anaerobic fermentation and
bile-acid-converting community structure after FMT. The
\texttt{Sutterella} depth-2 signal is interesting, but it is best
treated as exploratory until it can be tied more directly to recurrence,
bile acids, or treatment status.

\subsection{Lung disease}\label{lung-disease}


The gut-lung axis links microbial fermentation, immune tone, and airway
phenotypes, but the AGP ``lung disease'' label is broad and probably
pools several different conditions
(\citeproc{ref-Budden2017_gutlung}{Budden et al. 2017};
\citeproc{ref-Trompette2014_fiber_asthma}{Trompette et al. 2014};
\citeproc{ref-Horn2022_Prevotella_lung}{Horn et al. 2022}).


In SILVA, the main adjusted depth-1 lung-disease signal was
\texttt{Staphylococcus} enrichment (adjusted OR 1.55, q = 3.9e-02),
while univariate depletion appeared for \texttt{Prevotella\_7},
\texttt{Streptococcus}, and \texttt{Pasteurellaceae}. GG2 reproduced the
depleted \texttt{Prevotella} / \texttt{Streptococcus} /
\texttt{Pasteurellaceae} side of the signal. Depth 2 added
\texttt{Agathobacter} and \texttt{Staphylococcus} subtype associations.
None of the signals survived sensitivity analysis.


This phenotype is too broad to support a strong disease-specific claim,
and the comparison literature itself is split between gut-origin asthma
studies, airway COPD studies, and respiratory-infection models
(\citeproc{ref-Budden2017_gutlung}{Budden et al. 2017};
\citeproc{ref-Trompette2014_fiber_asthma}{Trompette et al. 2014};
\citeproc{ref-Horn2022_Prevotella_lung}{Horn et al. 2022}). The
surviving \texttt{Staphylococcus} signal is therefore difficult to
interpret as a gut-lung-axis result per se, especially because it
disappears in the clean analysis. The most defensible reading is that
the AGP screen detects a weak, partly compartment-mixed respiratory
signal rather than a coherent lung-disease microbiome signature.

\subsection{Migraine}\label{migraine}


Migraine is increasingly discussed through gut-brain signaling,
inflammation, and microbial metabolite effects, but the current
microbiome evidence base is thinner than for IBD or allergy
(\citeproc{ref-Cryan2012_gutbrain}{Cryan and Dinan 2012};
\citeproc{ref-Gorenshtein2025_migraine}{Gorenshtein et al. 2025};
\citeproc{ref-Kappeter2023_migraine_FMT}{Kappéter et al. 2023}).


Migraine produced one adjusted depth-1 signal in SILVA:
\texttt{Bacteroides} enrichment (adjusted OR 1.16, q = 3.4e-02). GG2
showed a qualitatively similar \texttt{Bacteroides}-side enrichment
together with weaker secondary signals. No depth-2 association reached
significance, and nothing survived the sensitivity analysis.


This remains exploratory. The direct migraine microbiome literature is
still small and heterogeneous, with current reviews emphasizing
dysbiosis, SCFA and barrier pathways, and gut-brain-axis signaling more
than any one reproducible taxon
(\citeproc{ref-Gorenshtein2025_migraine}{Gorenshtein et al. 2025};
\citeproc{ref-Kappeter2023_migraine_FMT}{Kappéter et al. 2023};
\citeproc{ref-Cryan2012_gutbrain}{Cryan and Dinan 2012}). The present
\texttt{Bacteroides} enrichment is therefore best read as a weak
cross-disease inflammatory echo rather than as migraine-specific
evidence.

\subsection{Kidney disease}\label{kidney-disease}


The gut-kidney axis is mechanistically attractive because microbial
metabolites such as TMAO, indoxyl sulfate, and p-cresyl sulfate can
worsen renal decline (\citeproc{ref-Tang2015_TMAO_CKD}{Tang et al.
2015}; \citeproc{ref-Vaziri2013_CKD}{Vaziri et al. 2013}).


Kidney disease showed no univariate depth-1 signals, but the adjusted
pass identified strong enrichment of \texttt{Lactobacillus} (adjusted OR
5.85, q = 7.8e-03) and \texttt{Prevotella\_7} (adjusted OR 4.57, q =
1.0e-02). GG2 recovered a compatible pattern through enriched
\texttt{Prevotella} and a reclassified \texttt{Bacteroides} group. Depth
2 retained a \texttt{Lactobacillus} signal, and both adjusted depth-1
associations survived sensitivity analysis.


Kidney disease offers a useful example of a signal that is statistically
stronger than its immediate biological label. The CKD literature is most
coherent at the mechanism layer: depletion of saccharolytic commensals,
accumulation of uremic-toxin pathways, and the broader
gut-kidney/cardiorenal axis built around TMAO, rather than any universal
\texttt{Lactobacillus} signature (\citeproc{ref-Tang2015_TMAO_CKD}{Tang
et al. 2015}; \citeproc{ref-Vaziri2013_CKD}{Vaziri et al. 2013}). The
observed \texttt{Lactobacillus} and \texttt{Prevotella\_7} enrichments
may therefore encode CKD stage, treatment behavior, probiotic use, or
compensatory ecology superimposed on a real gut-kidney-axis signal. What
keeps this section prominent is robustness: unlike several weaker
phenotypes, the adjusted depth-1 associations survive the clean
analysis. The manuscript should nonetheless place more weight on the
shared mechanism story than on any one AGP taxon label.

\section{Discussion}\label{general-discussion}

For submission, the manuscript's main claim should stay narrower than a
general disease atlas. The central result is that DCSTs convert a very
large gut microbiome table into explicit sample-level states that remain
interpretable after covariate adjustment, contamination-aware filtering,
taxonomy change, and partially heterogeneous external validation. The
phenotype-resolved organization matters because it separates strong
inflammatory signals from weaker or more fragile discovery outcomes
instead of flattening everything into one pooled odds-ratio screen.

Several cross-cutting patterns survive this disease-wise reading. First,
\texttt{Bacteroides}-dominant structure repeatedly aligns with
inflammatory or inflammation-adjacent phenotypes, including IBD,
autoimmune disease, acid reflux, and migraine. Second,
\texttt{Prevotella}-side dominance is repeatedly protective in allergy
and inflammatory settings, especially seasonal allergies and IBD. Third,
rare but striking enterobacterial states, particularly
\texttt{Morganella}, behave as markers of severe dysbiosis rather than
as subtle population shifts. Fourth, depth 2 adds real information by
turning a top-dominant label into an ordered ecological pair. For a
mathematically oriented reader, this is important: the refinement is
explicit and interpretable rather than latent.

Those broad patterns also line up with the stronger disease-specific
literatures in recognizable ways. In IBD, the combination of
enterobacterial rare states, \texttt{Prevotella\_9} depletion, and
depth-2 \texttt{Bacteroides} heterogeneity matches the treatment-naive
and multi-omics literature that emphasizes loss of butyrate- associated
anaerobes together with inflammatory expansion of facultative taxa
(\citeproc{ref-Gevers2014_CD}{Gevers et al. 2014};
\citeproc{ref-LloydPrice2019_IBD}{Lloyd-Price et al. 2019};
\citeproc{ref-Sokol2008_Fprausnitzii}{Sokol et al. 2008}). IBS shows a
weaker but directionally similar ecology, with Enterobacteriaceae-side
enrichment and \texttt{Faecalibacterium} depletion rather than a
dramatic dysbiosis collapse
(\citeproc{ref-Pittayanon2019_IBS}{Pittayanon et al. 2019};
\citeproc{ref-Tap2017_IBS}{Tap et al. 2017}). Seasonal allergy aligns
with the microbiota-maturation and immune-education literature, in which
reduced tolerogenic fermentation and delayed ecological maturation
accompany allergic phenotypes (\citeproc{ref-Hua2016_AGP_allergy}{Hua et
al. 2016}; \citeproc{ref-Hoskinson2023_allergy}{Hoskinson et al. 2023};
\citeproc{ref-Strachan1989_hygiene}{Strachan 1989}). CDI fits the
colonization-resistance framework of bile acids, donor-like recovery,
and Proteobacteria blooms (\citeproc{ref-Schubert2014_CDI}{Schubert et
al. 2014}; \citeproc{ref-Seekatz2014_FMT}{Seekatz et al. 2014};
\citeproc{ref-Sehgal2021_CDI}{Sehgal and Khanna 2021}). Kidney disease
is the least mechanistically settled of these stronger signals at the
taxon level, but it still sits plausibly within the broader
gut-kidney/cardiorenal axis defined by TMAO and uremic-toxin biology
(\citeproc{ref-Tang2015_TMAO_CKD}{Tang et al. 2015};
\citeproc{ref-Vaziri2013_CKD}{Vaziri et al. 2013}). Acid reflux is
informative in the opposite way: the surviving stool-side
\texttt{Bacteroides} association is usable, but mainly as a
compartment-aware signal within a literature that remains centered on
oral and esophageal microbiology rather than stool
(\citeproc{ref-Deshpande2018_esophageal}{Deshpande et al. 2018};
\citeproc{ref-DeSouza2021_GERD}{D'Souza et al. 2021};
\citeproc{ref-Wang2024_GERD_MR}{K. Wang et al. 2024}).

The expanded validation set sharpens the same picture. Strongest
completed replication concentrates in the inflammatory branch,
especially through HMP2 and Halfvarson, while PRJEB84421 and Gevers show
that ecological match and phenotype definition matter for whether that
signal survives q-correction. Outside IBD, the completed cohorts are
mostly null or near-null, with \texttt{GUBA-BD-2024} as the main
positive non-IBD example. That pattern is useful in itself: the DCST
pipeline generalizes across cohorts, but strong association replication
is selective rather than automatic.

Additional external cohorts are still being processed, and those
results should be inserted into the matching phenotype subsections in
the submission version rather than collected into a new standalone
validation section. A new cohort can strengthen the paper by showing
positive or directional replication, but a weak/null cohort is also
informative because it defines the ecological and phenotypic boundary of
the framework. The manuscript should therefore prefer an honest
scorecard of completed outcomes over a success-only validation
narrative.

The paper should still be careful about overclaiming. AGP phenotypes are
self-reported. Some disease bins are umbrella categories. The analysis
is cross-sectional, so reverse causation, medication effects, and
residual confounding remain live possibilities. The sensitivity analysis
shows that some signals rely on taxa that are plausibly oral or skin
associated. And the validation cohorts, while valuable, are
intentionally heterogeneous rather than perfect one-to-one replicas of
AGP stool. Those limitations argue for a measured conclusion: DCSTs
organize the data in a biologically useful way, but they do not by
themselves establish mechanism or causality. For the same reason, the
phenotype-specific literature discussion should be read as
mechanistic context for the discovery-screen phenotypes rather than as
proof that the AGP labels are clinically identical to those
literature-defined entities. Reflux is the clearest
compartment-sensitive example, and kidney disease is the clearest
treatment-sensitive example.

\section{Conclusion}\label{conclusion}

Taken together, this manuscript supports DCSTs as a reproducible
analysis layer for large gut microbiome cohorts. The strongest completed
case is inflammatory disease, especially IBD, where discovery,
subtype-level refinement, taxonomy concordance, and external validation
point in the same direction. The broader phenotype screen remains useful
because it shows which signals are durable and which collapse after
adjustment or contaminant-aware filtering. As additional external stool
cohorts finish processing, the key test will not be whether every AGP
result repeats exactly, but whether the same dominant-state architecture
and the same inflammatory-versus-fragile boundary recur across
independent datasets.
