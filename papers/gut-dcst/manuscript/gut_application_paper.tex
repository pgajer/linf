\section{Abstract}\label{abstract}

Inflammatory bowel disease (IBD) is associated with heterogeneous gut
microbiome dysbiosis, including loss or redistribution of obligate
anaerobic commensals and expansion of facultative or
inflammation-associated taxa. A practical
challenge is to summarize these shifts as sample-level ecological states
that remain interpretable across large cohorts. Gut enterotypes provide
one such summary, but they are clustering-defined constructs whose
labels depend on choices about data transformation, distance metric,
clustering model, and the number of clusters. We therefore applied
dominant-community state types (dCSTs), a deterministic rank-based
description of retained-feature dominance, to 30,290 quality-controlled
American Gut Project stool samples from a 34,711-sample SILVA
species-level table. The strongest disease-contextual signal was
self-reported IBD. A broad \taxon{Bacteroides} enrichment at depth 1
resolved into progressively stronger \taxon{Bacteroides}-centered dCST
lineages at depths 2 to 4, while Bayesian models retained finite
estimates for exploratory rare enterobacterial states. The IBD signal was
robust to a stricter control definition, and external validation
separated two forms of reproducibility: Halfvarson 2017 supported the IBD
branch in both rebuilt-cohort and frozen-transfer analyses, PRJEB84421
provided narrower rebuilt inflammatory support, and pooled HMP2 retained
a weaker transferred depth-2 IBD signal after explicit case coding. IBS
and autoimmune disease showed weaker but directionally related dominance
patterns. These results support dCSTs as a simple,
biologist-readable tool for resolving IBD-associated gut microbiome
dominance structure, with broader gut-outcome applications remaining more
exploratory.

\textbf{Keywords:} gut microbiome; inflammatory bowel disease;
dysbiosis; enterotypes; dominant-community state types; Bayesian
modeling

\section{Introduction}\label{introduction}

IBD is a useful biological setting for testing whether sample-level
dominance states can clarify gut microbiome dysbiosis. Landmark studies
have shown that IBD is not characterized by one universal microbial
signature; rather, it often involves reduced diversity, depletion of
butyrate-associated obligate anaerobes, and expansion of facultative or
inflammation-associated taxa
(\citeproc{ref-Frank2007_IBD}{Frank et al. 2007};
\citeproc{ref-Gevers2014_CD}{Gevers et al. 2014};
\citeproc{ref-LloydPrice2019_IBD}{Lloyd-Price et al. 2019};
\citeproc{ref-Sokol2008_Fprausnitzii}{Sokol et al. 2008};
\citeproc{ref-Manichanh2006_CD}{Manichanh et al. 2006}). These changes
are biologically meaningful but difficult to express as simple labels
that can be assigned to individual samples and compared across cohorts.

Gut enterotypes were introduced to provide such labels by compressing
high-dimensional gut abundance profiles into a small number of
clustering-defined configurations
(\citeproc{ref-Arumugam2011_enterotypes}{Arumugam et al. 2011}). That
abstraction can be informative for broad community structure, but it has
remained controversial precisely because the labels are clustering
constructs: assignments depend on data transformation, distance metric,
initialization, model class, and the chosen number of clusters. Those
choices can make it hard to determine what a label means biologically
for a particular sample or whether labels from different analyses are
directly comparable.

We use dominant-community state types (dCSTs) as a deliberately simple
alternative way to name sample-level gut ecological states. A dCST is
defined by the within-sample ordering of retained taxa. At depth 1, the
state is assigned by the dominant retained taxon; at depth 2, it is
refined by the ordered pair of the two most abundant retained taxa.
Because assignment depends only on within-sample rank order, the same
label is recovered from counts, proportions, or any sample-wide
rescaling that preserves taxon order within a sample. A depth-\(\ell\)
dCST label is a dCST lineage: an ordered path through this refinement
tree, not a phylogenetic lineage. The dCST construction therefore does
not claim to replace mechanistic microbiome modeling. Its purpose here
is more practical: to ask whether IBD-associated dysbiosis can be
summarized as transparent dominance states that remain readable after
covariate adjustment, hierarchical refinement, Bayesian sparse-state
follow-up, and external validation.

We center the manuscript on IBD because it produced the strongest and
most reproducible biological signal in the American Gut Project (AGP)
screen. We also report IBS and autoimmune disease as related gut or
immune-associated outcomes because literature suggests that shifts in
dominant taxa or dominant taxon pairs can mark altered ecological states
in those settings
(\citeproc{ref-Tap2017_IBS}{Tap et al. 2017};
\citeproc{ref-Pittayanon2019_IBS}{Pittayanon et al. 2019};
\citeproc{ref-Scher2013_Prevotella_RA}{Scher et al. 2013};
\citeproc{ref-Vatanen2018_T1D}{Vatanen et al. 2018}). We use
``inflammatory'' as a disease-contextual and literature-grounded
descriptor rather than as evidence that host inflammatory biomarkers were
measured in the AGP discovery cohort. In this study we use the absorb
low-frequency policy, which merges very small dominance patterns into
larger named states rather than pooling them into one catch-all rare
category. Pure-policy dCST results are used only as a baseline
comparison in the supplement rather than as a second main-text results
stream.

\section{Materials and Methods}\label{methods}

The discovery analysis used AGP gut samples from the PRIME-derived SILVA
species-level abundance table (\citeproc{ref-McDonald2018_AGP}{McDonald
et al. 2018}; \citeproc{ref-Quast2013_SILVA}{Quast et al. 2013}). We
restricted the analysis to the 34,711 AGP rows present in the PRIME
gut-project abundance table. Samples with total library size below
1,000 reads were excluded, removing 4,421 samples.
Taxa were then retained only if they reached a count of at least 2 in at
least 5\% of the retained samples, corresponding to a prevalence
threshold of 1,515 samples in this cohort. This feature filter retained
274 taxa, and no additional samples became all-zero after feature
filtering, leaving 30,290 samples for the discovery analysis. Age was
available for 27,372 samples (90.4\%), sex for 25,598 (84.5\%), and BMI
for 26,541 (87.6\%), producing 23,198 complete cases for the absorb
follow-up models. Because dCST construction was applied after this
feature-retention step, all dCST labels should be read as
retained-feature dominance labels. They do not imply dominance over every
raw feature removed during preprocessing. Feature labels use the deepest
informative SILVA annotation available for each retained feature, so
labels can refer to species, genera, families, orders, or unresolved
features such as \emph{Unassigned}.

Phenotype flags were derived directly from the AGP \texttt{Phenotype}
metadata field by fixed substring matching. The focal outcomes were
coded from the phrases \texttt{irritable bowel syndrome},
\texttt{inflammatory bowel disease}, \texttt{autoimmune},
\texttt{acid reflux}, and \texttt{seasonal allergies}. Samples without
the focal phrase served as controls for that outcome, and outcome flags
were therefore not mutually exclusive. In the filtered cohort, case
counts were 3,105 for IBS, 1,166 for IBD, 2,872 for autoimmune disease,
2,859 for acid reflux, and 9,493 for seasonal allergies; corresponding
complete-case counts after requiring age, sex, and BMI were 2,551, 993,
2,426, 2,279, and 8,284, respectively (Supplementary Table S1). For the
AGP discovery cohort, IBD subtype, disease activity, medication exposure,
and recent antibiotic exposure were not consistently available in a form
that supported reliable adjustment; the discovery IBD analyses are
therefore interpreted as self-reported phenotype associations rather than
as treatment-naive clinical subtype analyses.

Absorb-policy dCSTs were constructed from the within-sample dominance order. A
depth-1 state is defined by the dominant retained taxon, and deeper states are
defined by recursively appending the second-ranked, third-ranked, and
subsequent retained taxa. Because assignment depends only on within-sample rank
order, the label is unchanged whether the input is expressed as raw
counts, relative abundances, or any other sample-wide rescaling that
preserves the order of taxa within each sample. We used the
\texttt{absorb} low-frequency policy throughout. At depth 1, only
dominance patterns observed in at least \(n_0 = 50\) samples were kept
as named states. Samples in smaller patterns were reassigned to the
retained state with the largest abundance among the retained taxa for
that sample, with deterministic tie handling. We then refined the
hierarchy with \(n_0 = 25, 12, 10, 8,\) and \(5\) for depths 2 through
6, respectively. At each depth, the same rule was applied within the
current parent state, producing ordered dCST lineages. Depths 1
through 4 are treated as the primary results in this manuscript, with
deeper levels used mainly to judge whether the hierarchy remained
structured.

The pure-policy dCST hierarchy without absorption served as a baseline
comparison and is summarized in the supplement. The inferential workflow
reported here is therefore absorb based from the cohort overview
through phenotype-specific follow-up.

To ask whether a phenotype was associated with the overall absorb-state
distribution at a given depth, we first applied Fisher's exact test to
the phenotype-by-state table for that depth. We screened panels with
10,000 Monte Carlo draws and reran the strongest panels with 100,000
draws to obtain finer p-value resolution. We summarize the magnitude of
these depth-level associations with Cramer's V. We then performed
label-level follow-up in two parallel ways for IBD, IBS, autoimmune
disease, acid reflux, and seasonal allergies. The conventional analysis used Fisher
tests and logistic regression adjusted for age, sex, and BMI. The
Bayesian analysis used two complementary models. For unadjusted
label-level contrasts we used a beta-binomial prevalence model with a
Jeffreys prior, \(\mathrm{Beta}(0.5, 0.5)\), summarized by the
posterior median odds ratio, 95\% credible interval, and posterior
probability that the odds ratio exceeded 1. For adjusted analyses we fit
Bayesian logistic regression models with \texttt{arm::bayesglm} using
the same covariates as the frequentist regression and the package's
default weakly informative prior. Those adjusted Bayesian fits were
summarized by the exponentiated coefficient estimate, an approximate
95\% credible interval based on the posterior standard error, and
\(\Pr(\beta > 0)\) for enrichment. Multiple testing in the conventional
analyses was controlled with the Benjamini-Hochberg procedure
(\citeproc{ref-BenjaminiHochberg1995}{Benjamini and Hochberg 1995}).

The results presented in detail here are the branches that combined the
clearest absorb-hierarchy structure with stable Bayesian follow-up: IBD,
IBS, and autoimmune disease. Additional non-IBD branches are summarized
in the supplement. As a control-definition sensitivity check for the
anchor IBD branch, we repeated the IBD follow-up after excluding non-IBD
controls with the other focal AGP phenotype strings. External validation
was evaluated in two complementary ways. In rebuilt-cohort validation,
each external matrix
was filtered and analyzed independently with the same absorb workflow
used for the discovery cohort. In frozen-transfer validation, external
samples were mapped directly onto the AGP-derived absorb hierarchy after
aligning each cohort's taxa to the AGP retained-feature dictionary. The
completed validation cohorts included HMP2 / IBDMDB, Halfvarson 2017,
Gevers 2014, PRJEB84421, and Jacobs 2023 IBS 250bp; the main text
emphasizes the IBD-focused cohorts because they currently provide the
clearest external readout, and the full comparison summary is given in
Supplementary Table S4. All manuscript figures, tables, and
analysis scripts were generated from workflows built around the
\texttt{linf} R package, with project-specific runners maintained in
the companion \texttt{gut\_microbiome} repository.

\section{Results}\label{cohort-overview}

\subsection{Discovery screen identifies IBD as the strongest
dominance-state signal}\label{shared-agp-overview}

The discovery analysis asked whether interpretable dominance states
could recover disease-associated gut ecology in 30,290
quality-controlled AGP stool samples. The absorb dCST hierarchy
contained 40 named states at depth 1, 153 at depth 2, 479 at depth 3,
and 744 at depth 4. The largest depth-1 states were
\taxon{Bacteroides} (10,599 samples),
\taxon{Escherichia-Shigella} (6,913), \taxon{Prevotella\_9} (1,926),
Enterobacteriaceae (1,596), and \taxon{Faecalibacterium} (1,405). The
twelve most common depth-1 absorb states covered 87.3\% of the cohort
(Supplementary Table S5), showing that a limited number of dominant
ecological configurations accounted for most samples even after
low-frequency patterns were absorbed into larger named states. Some
retained-feature labels also carried low-resolution or potential non-gut
annotations, including
\emph{Unassigned}, \taxon{Pseudomonas}, \taxon{Staphylococcus}, and, at
deeper levels, \emph{Eukaryota}/\emph{Unassigned}/Bacteria/Mitochondria
(Supplementary Tables S2 and S5). We retained these labels in the
unsupervised hierarchy for transparent accounting, but they are not used
as biological anchors for the IBD interpretation.

The depthwise phenotype screen then identified IBD as the clearest
biological signal. Across depths 1 to 4, Cramer's V increased from 0.11
to 0.35 in IBD, compared with 0.11 to 0.28 in autoimmune disease and
0.08 to 0.19 in IBS. This pattern suggested that the broad IBD
association at shallow depth was being refined into more informative
substates at greater depth. The absorb policy mattered most at these
deeper levels: relative to the pure hierarchy, only 3.8\% of depth-1
assignments changed, but 18.5\% of samples were reassigned at depth 2,
52.0\% at depth 3, and 71.6\% at depth 4. Biologically, absorption kept
larger named lineages available for interpretation instead of allowing a
growing fraction of samples to disappear into a residual rare-state tail
(Supplementary Table S2). We therefore focus detailed follow-up on IBD,
with IBS and autoimmune disease retained as related secondary branches.
Two additional non-IBD examples are summarized in the supplement rather
than developed as main-text branches (Supplementary Table S6).

The phenotype-wide absorb screen remained broad, with omnibus signal
across several outcomes at depths 1 through 4. However, IBD combined the
largest depthwise growth in effect size, the clearest lineage-level
interpretability, and the strongest external validation. IBS and
autoimmune disease are therefore presented as secondary gut or
immune-associated extensions of the IBD-centered pattern.

\subsection{Inflammatory bowel disease
(IBD)}\label{inflammatory-bowel-disease-ibd}


Self-reported IBD provides the most clinically grounded inflammatory
phenotype in the AGP screen, even though the discovery metadata do not
separate treatment-naive Crohn disease, ulcerative colitis, disease
activity, and medication exposure. Landmark studies report reduced diversity, expansion of
Proteobacteria, and depletion of butyrate-associated commensals,
especially \emph{Faecalibacterium prausnitzii}
(\citeproc{ref-Frank2007_IBD}{Frank et al. 2007};
\citeproc{ref-Gevers2014_CD}{Gevers et al. 2014};
\citeproc{ref-LloydPrice2019_IBD}{Lloyd-Price et al. 2019};
\citeproc{ref-Sokol2008_Fprausnitzii}{Sokol et al. 2008};
\citeproc{ref-Manichanh2006_CD}{Manichanh et al. 2006}).

IBD produced the strongest absorb hierarchy in the cohort. In the
conservative adjusted depth-1 screen, the clearest frequentist signals
were enrichment of \taxon{Bacteroides} (adjusted OR 1.54, q =
8.1e-10), \taxon{Bifidobacterium} (adjusted OR 3.38, q =
2.2e-04), Enterobacterales (adjusted OR 3.23, q = 0.0016), and
Lachnospiraceae (adjusted OR 1.99, q = 0.0030), together with
depletion of \taxon{Prevotella\_9} (adjusted OR 0.44, q =
2.2e-04). The Bayesian layer recovered the same common-state picture,
but it also highlighted very rare enterobacterial states that were too
small for the conservative adjusted frequentist screen. In particular,
\taxon{Morganella} showed a Bayesian adjusted OR of 18.99
(95\% credible interval 10.15--35.53) and \taxon{Proteus} showed a
Bayesian adjusted OR of 3.40 (95\% credible interval 1.40--8.27), both
with near-complete posterior support for enrichment in IBD. These
rare-state examples were small: \taxon{Morganella} contained 22 IBD
samples and 34 controls, and \taxon{Proteus} contained 6 IBD samples and
45 controls. We therefore treat them as biologically suggestive
rare-state signals rather than as stand-alone biomarkers.

The value of the absorb hierarchy became even clearer at greater depth.
At depth 2, \taxon{Bacteroides}/Lachnospiraceae reached adjusted OR
3.21 (q = 2.6e-33), \taxon{Bacteroides}/[\emph{Eubacterium}]
\emph{eligens} group reached adjusted OR 22.50 (q = 5.0e-24), and
\taxon{Bacteroides}/Enterobacteriaceae reached adjusted OR 2.73 (q =
2.2e-04). At depth 3, the strongest lineage was
\taxon{Bacteroides}/Lachnospiraceae/\taxon{Alistipes} (adjusted OR
8.56, q = 1.5e-28), followed by
\taxon{Bacteroides}/\emph{[Eubacterium]\_eligens\_group}/Lachnospiraceae
(adjusted OR 22.50, q = 5.0e-24) and
\taxon{Bacteroides}/\taxon{Alistipes}/Lachnospiraceae (adjusted OR
5.95, q = 6.1e-15). At depth 4, the best supported lineage remained
\taxon{Bacteroides}/Lachnospiraceae/\taxon{Alistipes}/\taxon{Faecalibacterium}
(adjusted OR 5.53, q = 1.1e-07). For a biologist, the practical reading
is simple: the broad depth-1 \taxon{Bacteroides} state contains several
very different IBD-associated sub-lineages, and the absorb policy keeps
those lineages named and interpretable instead of sending them into a
generic rare bucket. A dCST lineage label records rank context rather
than a claim that every named component is enriched in IBD. For example,
the depth-4
\taxon{Bacteroides}/Lachnospiraceae/\taxon{Alistipes}/\taxon{Faecalibacterium}
state remains a \taxon{Bacteroides}-first retained-feature context;
\taxon{Faecalibacterium} is a lower-ranked component within that label
(Supplementary Table S8). This distinction matters because the broader
IBD literature often reports depletion of \emph{F. prausnitzii} or
butyrate-associated commensals.

The IBD result was also robust to a stricter control definition. After
removing non-IBD controls who carried any of the other focal AGP
phenotype strings, the analysis retained all 1,166 IBD cases and 16,175
cleaner controls. The broad \taxon{Bacteroides} signal remained enriched
(adjusted OR 1.59, q = 1.1e-10), as did the
\taxon{Bacteroides}/Lachnospiraceae lineage at depth 2 (adjusted OR
3.15, q = 1.5e-28) and the
\taxon{Bacteroides}/Lachnospiraceae/\taxon{Alistipes} lineage at depth 3
(adjusted OR 10.15, q = 6.2e-22). The depth-4
\taxon{Bacteroides}/Lachnospiraceae/\taxon{Alistipes}/\taxon{Faecalibacterium}
lineage retained a finite Bayesian estimate but became too small for the
conservative adjusted frequentist screen. The rare \taxon{Morganella}
and \taxon{Proteus} states likewise fell below the prespecified support
threshold in this sensitivity analysis, reinforcing the need to interpret
them cautiously (Supplementary Table S7).

External validation asked two related but distinct questions. Rebuilt
cohort validation asked whether the absorb dCST analysis rediscovered
disease-associated dominant states when each external dataset was
analyzed independently. Frozen-transfer validation then asked whether
AGP-defined absorb states themselves could be assigned to external
samples and retain signal without rebuilding the hierarchy.

Across the completed inflammatory cohorts, rebuilt-cohort validation was
strongest in Halfvarson 2017, where the \texttt{IBD\_vs\_Healthy},
\texttt{Crohn\_vs\_Healthy}, and \texttt{UC\_vs\_Healthy} comparisons
all remained significant after multiple-testing correction. The leading
rebuilt labels differed across those contrasts but pointed to the same
broad inflammatory axis: depletion of \taxon{Segatella} sp. in pooled
IBD and Crohn disease together with enrichment of \taxon{Bacteroides}
\emph{dorei} in ulcerative colitis. PRJEB84421 provided narrower
support through
\texttt{OFG\_vs\_Healthy}, where a depth-2
\taxon{Faecalibacterium}\dcstsep\taxon{Bacteroides} lineage was depleted
(q = 0.019). By contrast, rebuilt \texttt{IBD\_vs\_Healthy} in HMP2 and
rebuilt \texttt{Crohn\_vs\_Healthy} in Gevers 2014 remained directional
but did not retain BH-significant absorb states, which is not
surprising given repeated sampling, substantial clinical heterogeneity,
and the more specialized ecology of those cohorts.

Frozen transfer was stricter but still informative. Halfvarson remained
clearly positive when samples were mapped directly onto AGP-defined
states: pooled IBD, Crohn, and UC comparisons all retained q < 0.001,
with the transferred signal centered on enriched \taxon{Bacteroides}
states and depleted \taxon{Faecalibacterium}-linked states. After
explicitly coding pooled HMP2 inflammatory bowel disease as one case
label, HMP2 also retained a weaker but significant depth-2 transferred
\taxon{Bacteroides}\dcstsep\taxon{Faecalibacterium} depletion in
\texttt{IBD\_vs\_Healthy} (q = 0.027). Gevers 2014 and PRJEB84421 both
mapped well into the AGP hierarchy but did not retain BH-significant
transferred states, and the current Jacobs IBS cohort was likewise
directional at most. We therefore interpret the present external data as
strongest support for IBD-associated dominance-pattern reproducibility,
with exact label portability clearly demonstrated in Halfvarson and
supported more selectively in pooled HMP2 (Figure 4; Supplementary Table
S4).

These IBD results align well with the established literature. The
classic IBD story is not that one taxon always dominates, but that
protective obligate anaerobes are lost while inflammatory facultative
taxa expand (\citeproc{ref-Frank2007_IBD}{Frank et al. 2007};
\citeproc{ref-Gevers2014_CD}{Gevers et al. 2014};
\citeproc{ref-LloydPrice2019_IBD}{Lloyd-Price et al. 2019};
\citeproc{ref-Sokol2008_Fprausnitzii}{Sokol et al. 2008};
\citeproc{ref-Sartor2008_IBD}{Sartor 2008}). The absorb-policy dCST analysis
compresses that pattern into an interpretable hierarchy: a broad
\taxon{Bacteroides} background, IBD-associated
\taxon{Bacteroides}-centered lineages at greater depth, and a small set
of rare enterobacterial states that may mark more severe dysbiosis.

\subsection{Irritable bowel syndrome
(IBS)}\label{irritable-bowel-syndrome-ibs}


IBS provides a useful secondary context for the approach because its gut
microbiome signal is typically subtler than in overt inflammatory bowel
disease
(\citeproc{ref-Pittayanon2019_IBS}{Pittayanon et al. 2019};
\citeproc{ref-Dinan2015_IBS}{Dinan and Cryan 2015};
\citeproc{ref-Cryan2012_gutbrain}{Cryan and Dinan 2012}). In the absorb
hierarchy, IBS showed a moderate but internally consistent shift toward
enterobacterial dominance. At depth 1,
\taxon{Escherichia-Shigella} was enriched in IBS (adjusted OR 1.24, q =
0.002), while \taxon{Staphylococcus}, \taxon{Faecalibacterium}, and
\taxon{Prevotella\_9} were depleted. At depth 2, two
\taxon{Escherichia-Shigella}/\taxon{Bacteroides}-centered states
remained significant, and by depth 3 the clearest surviving frequentist
signal was an
\taxon{Escherichia-Shigella}/\taxon{Bacteroides}/Lachnospiraceae
lineage (adjusted OR 1.78, q = 0.018).

The Bayesian follow-up preserved the same ecological pattern at greater
depth, with strong posterior support for depletion of
\taxon{Faecalibacterium}- and \taxon{Prevotella\_9}-linked states and
for enrichment of deeper
\taxon{Escherichia-Shigella}/\taxon{Bacteroides}/Lachnospiraceae
lineages even when the frequentist q-values became sparse. Biologically,
the IBS branch therefore reads as a milder dysbiosis-associated
dominance shift rather than as a fully distinct ecological program,
which is compatible with the current IBS literature
(\citeproc{ref-Pittayanon2019_IBS}{Pittayanon et al. 2019};
\citeproc{ref-Tap2017_IBS}{Tap et al. 2017}).

\subsection{Autoimmune diseases}\label{autoimmune-diseases}


The AGP autoimmune phenotype is an umbrella label rather than a single
disease and therefore requires more cautious interpretation than IBD.
Even so,
autoimmune disease is an important category because the literature
repeatedly links loss of immune-regulatory commensals and expansion of
pathobionts to altered Treg/Th17 balance
(\citeproc{ref-Scher2013_Prevotella_RA}{Scher et al. 2013};
\citeproc{ref-Atarashi2013_Clostridia_Treg}{Atarashi et al. 2013};
\citeproc{ref-DeLuca2019_autoimmune}{De Luca and Shoenfeld 2019};
\citeproc{ref-Vatanen2018_T1D}{Vatanen et al. 2018}).

Autoimmune disease showed an absorb pattern compatible with
inflammation-associated dysbiosis but more heterogeneous than IBD. At depth 1, the dominant
signals were enrichment of \taxon{Bacteroides} (adjusted OR 1.40, q =
1.6e-12) together with depletion of \taxon{Prevotella\_9},
\taxon{Streptococcus}, \taxon{Faecalibacterium}, and
\taxon{Staphylococcus}. At depth 2, the hierarchy sharpened around
\taxon{Bacteroides}-centered lineages:
\taxon{Bacteroides}/Lachnospiraceae reached adjusted OR 2.15 (q =
5.1e-22), \taxon{Bacteroides}/[\emph{Eubacterium}]
\emph{eligens\_group} reached adjusted OR 21.95 (q = 3.2e-20), and
\taxon{Bacteroides}/\taxon{Alistipes} reached adjusted OR 1.70 (q =
2.5e-05). The same branch continued deeper, with
\taxon{Bacteroides}/Lachnospiraceae/\taxon{Alistipes} reaching adjusted
OR 5.52 (q = 3.9e-21) at depth 3 and
\taxon{Bacteroides}/Lachnospiraceae/\taxon{Alistipes}/\taxon{Faecalibacterium}
remaining significant at depth 4 (adjusted OR 3.44, q = 6.1e-05).

The Bayesian analysis again helped mostly at the deeper levels. It
supported the same major \taxon{Bacteroides}-centered lineages as the
frequentist analysis, but it also retained support for additional depth-4
states such as
\taxon{Bacteroides}/\taxon{Akkermansia}/Lachnospiraceae/\taxon{Faecalibacterium}
and depleted
\taxon{Bacteroides}/\taxon{Faecalibacterium}/Lachnospiraceae/\taxon{Agathobacter}
states that no longer crossed the conventional q-value threshold. The
biological reading is therefore similar to IBD but more cautious: this
umbrella phenotype appears to be enriched for an inflammatory
\taxon{Bacteroides}-centered ecology, yet the pooled autoimmune label is
too broad to support one disease-specific mechanistic claim. The safest
interpretation is a shared inflammatory ecological axis rather than one
causal autoimmune taxon
(\citeproc{ref-Scher2013_Prevotella_RA}{Scher et al. 2013};
\citeproc{ref-Vatanen2018_T1D}{Vatanen et al. 2018};
\citeproc{ref-Atarashi2013_Clostridia_Treg}{Atarashi et al. 2013};
\citeproc{ref-DeLuca2019_autoimmune}{De Luca and Shoenfeld 2019}).

\section{Discussion}\label{general-discussion}

This study identifies IBD-associated gut dominance states that are
consistent with established dysbiosis biology and interpretable at the
level of individual samples. The central pattern is a broad
\taxon{Bacteroides} background that resolves into deeper
\taxon{Bacteroides}-centered lineages, together with sparse
enterobacterial states that may mark more severe dysbiosis but remain
exploratory because of their small support. This is not a claim that
dominant taxa alone explain IBD. Rather, the dCST labels provide a
compact way to name recurrent ecological contexts in which IBD cases are
overrepresented.

The analytical choices matter because they preserve that biological
readability. Absorbing very small dominant states into
larger named states is not a cosmetic change: at depth 1, the absorb and
pure views remain fairly close, but at depths 2 through 4 absorption
preserves interpretable named lineages instead of sending a growing
fraction of samples into a residual tail. The parallel Bayesian layer is
similarly useful not because it replaces standard testing, but because it
keeps estimates finite when biologically interesting states become
sparse. In practice, the frequentist and Bayesian analyses agreed
closely for common IBD-associated states, while the Bayesian models
remained informative for smaller deeper or rare enterobacterial states.

The secondary branches are best read as related gut-outcome context
rather than as co-equal discoveries. IBS was weaker but directionally
similar, with a shift toward \taxon{Escherichia-Shigella}-centered
states and away from \taxon{Faecalibacterium}- and
\taxon{Prevotella\_9}-linked states. Autoimmune disease showed a
parallel but broader inflammation-associated
\taxon{Bacteroides}-centered structure, which is plausible as a pooled
immune-dysregulation signal even though the AGP autoimmune label is too
heterogeneous for one disease-specific interpretation. Additional
non-IBD branches were also detected, but we treat them as supplementary
exploratory examples rather than as pillars of the main submission
story.

Several limitations remain important. External validation clarifies both
the promise and the current boundary of the IBD-centered claim. When
external cohorts are rebuilt independently, IBD-related dominance
structure is recovered most clearly in Halfvarson and more narrowly in
PRJEB84421, whereas HMP2 and Gevers contribute only mixed or directional
rebuilt evidence. When the AGP hierarchy is frozen and transferred
directly, Halfvarson remains clearly positive and pooled HMP2 retains a
weaker depth-2 transfer signal, but the other cohorts mainly demonstrate
mapping coverage rather than BH-significant label transfer. We therefore
interpret the current evidence as strong for IBD-focused
dominance-pattern reproducibility, moderate for portability of selected AGP-defined
inflammatory states, and still limited for broad claims about a
universally transferable label dictionary. Exact label transferability
will need broader stool-based validation, especially for IBS and any
non-IBD extensions of this approach. AGP phenotypes are also
self-reported and cross-sectional, so
residual confounding, medication effects, antibiotic exposure, and
reverse causation remain plausible. The dCST labels are also
retained-feature labels constructed after prevalence filtering, and some
low-resolution or potential non-gut labels appear in the full hierarchy;
those states are useful for transparent accounting but require caution
as biological signals. These concerns are especially important for
supplementary non-IBD phenotypes. The current evidence nevertheless
supports absorb-policy dCSTs as a biologically interpretable analysis
layer and motivates broader testing of their generalizability in
independent cohorts.

\section{Conclusion}\label{conclusion}

Taken together, these results support the presence of IBD-associated
dominance-state structure in the human gut microbiome. Absorb-policy
dCSTs provide the practical tool that makes this structure easy to name:
a broad dominant state resolves into biologically readable deeper
lineages, and cross-cohort support is already visible most clearly in
Halfvarson and more selectively in pooled HMP2 and PRJEB84421. The
parallel Bayesian analysis strengthens that picture by stabilizing rarer
deeper states without changing the main biological story. Secondary IBS
and autoimmune results suggest that the same approach can extend beyond
the anchor IBD branch, while broader non-IBD examples are best treated as
supplementary and hypothesis-generating until stronger external support
is available.
