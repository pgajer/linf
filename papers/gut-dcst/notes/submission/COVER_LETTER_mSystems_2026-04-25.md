Dear Editors of `mSystems`,

We are pleased to submit our manuscript, “Dominant-Community State Types Provide an Interpretable and Portable Representation of IBD-Associated Gut Microbiome Dysbiosis,” for consideration as a Research Article in `mSystems`.

This study addresses a systems-level problem in microbiome analysis: how to convert high-dimensional community profiles into sample-level states that remain interpretable and portable across cohorts. We develop dominant-community state types (dCSTs), a deterministic rank-based framework that labels samples by retained-feature dominance order rather than by fitting a clustering model. Using 30,290 quality-controlled American Gut Project stool samples as a structure-learning cohort, we derive an absorb-policy dCST hierarchy and then test whether that fixed state vocabulary remains informative in external inflammatory bowel disease cohorts with stronger diagnosis metadata.

The manuscript makes three main contributions that we believe fit `mSystems` well:

1. It introduces and applies a deterministic state-representation framework for microbial community data that avoids re-fitting cohort-specific clusters.
2. It shows that an AGP-derived state hierarchy can transfer into clinically annotated external IBD cohorts, with strongest sample-level support in Halfvarson 2017, selective pooled-motif support in a mucosal HMP2 slice, and a clear portability boundary in pediatric new-onset Gevers 2014.
3. It identifies an important limitation of fixed state vocabularies: rebuilt within-disease Crohn-location structure is stronger than AGP-frozen transfer for that subtype contrast, which helps define where cross-cohort transport is and is not adequate.

We view the manuscript as a systems-microbiology contribution rather than a biomarker or causality paper. Its central value is a reusable, interpretable state vocabulary for microbial communities, together with an empirical test of cross-cohort portability and its limits. We think this is well aligned with `mSystems`’ interest in insights drawn from large data sets and in computational approaches that yield interpretable views of microbial systems.

The work is original, is not under consideration elsewhere, and all authors have approved its submission. The manuscript source and paper assets are maintained in the public `linf` repository, and the project-specific runners used for discovery and external validation are maintained in the public `gut_microbiome` repository.
